## Abstract

Personality-adaptive conversational AI represents a paradigm shift from generic to individualized digital mental health support, yet most systems overlook stable trait differences that shape emotional processing and regulation needs. This study demonstrates architectural feasibility of a personality-adaptive framework integrating real-time Big Five trait detection with Zurich Model-aligned behavioral regulation under controlled simulation conditions.

We conducted a 2×2 factorial study comparing personality-adaptive (regulated) assistants against non-adaptive baselines across two extreme Big Five profiles (Type A: all traits +1; Type B: all traits −1) using GPT-4-based modules for detection, regulation, and evaluation. We generated 20 dialogue sessions (5 per cell in the 2×2 design), each consisting of six user–assistant exchanges (120 dialogue turns total). Evaluation assessed detection accuracy, regulation effectiveness, emotional tone, relevance, and personality-specific needs using an LLM evaluator benchmarked for agreement with human ratings (Cohen's κ = 0.89).

Under idealized conditions, the system achieved complete implementation fidelity (detection: 100%; regulation: 100%) and produced a selective enhancement pattern: personality-specific need fulfillment improved dramatically (d = 4.58, p < 0.001) while basic conversational quality remained equivalent (d < 0.3, ns). LLM-based evaluation was validated against human experts (κ = 0.89). These results represent an upper bound under controlled simulation; extensive human validation is required before clinical deployment.

This simulation establishes architectural feasibility of the proposed detection-regulation-evaluation pipeline using current LLM technology. By demonstrating that theoretically grounded adaptation outperforms generic approaches, this work opens pathways toward personalized digital mental health interventions while delineating critical validation requirements for safe clinical translation.

**Keywords:** digital mental health; conversational agents; large language models; personality detection; Big Five; Zurich Model; adaptive interaction; emotional support; simulation study; GPT-4


# 1. Introduction

Mental health disorders have surged globally despite economic and technological progress, with loneliness contributing to morbidity risks comparable to obesity [1]. Population aging, dispersed families, and declining community engagement create unprecedented demand for scalable digital mental health interventions [2,3].

Digital mental health tools leveraging large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated capacity for emotionally relevant, context-aware responses [8,9], yet a critical limitation persists: current systems rely on generic communication strategies that fail to account for stable personality differences shaping how individuals process emotions, seek support, and respond to intervention styles [4]. Existing approaches provide only shallow personalization through static assessments, lacking mechanisms for continuous personality modeling grounded in psychological theory or dynamic adaptation throughout interactions.

To address these limitations, we developed a personality-adaptive conversational framework integrating: (i) real-time Big Five personality trait detection using continuous linguistic analysis, (ii) behavior regulation grounded in the Zurich Model of Social Motivation, explicitly mapping traits to security, arousal, and affiliation domains, and (iii) structured LLM-based evaluation validated against human expert judgment. Implemented using GPT-4 and the PROMISE orchestration system [7], which ensures deterministic prompt control and traceable state transitions, the framework performs turn-by-turn personality inference and theory-guided behavioral adaptation throughout multi-turn conversations.

This controlled simulation study tests whether real-time personality-adaptive regulation produces measurable improvements in AI-to-AI simulation compared to non-adaptive baselines across boundary-condition personality profiles. This design provides precise experimental control for quantifying personalization effects, establishes a reproducible methodological testbed, and validates the implementation approach as a foundation for future human subject research toward safe AI-augmented mental health interventions.


# 2. Related Work

Personality-adaptive conversational AI represents a convergence of AI-driven interventions with personality psychology and affective computing. We review three interconnected streams: (1) affective computing and emotion-aware systems, (2) conversational AI architectures including transformer-based LLMs, and (3) personality psychology theories explaining individual differences in emotional processing.

## 2.1. Affective Computing and Emotion Recognition

Early affective computing established techniques for recognizing emotional states from text, speech, and facial expressions [12,13], evolving from categorical emotion models toward dimensional representations modeling valence and arousal. A critical limitation is emphasis on transient emotional states rather than stable personality traits, focusing on what users currently feel without considering how stable personality differences shape emotional processing and regulation needs [6].

## 2.2. Large Language Models in Mental Health

Large language models have transformed conversational AI, enabling flexible reasoning, context integration, and natural language understanding [8,15,16]. Transformer-based models implement cognitive functions through learned statistical patterns, supporting therapeutic applications via dialogue state tracking [15], cascaded empathy generation [16], and multimodal integration [17,19,20].

Despite these advances, current LLM-based therapeutic systems exhibit "one-size-fits-all" limitations. While generating contextually appropriate empathetic responses, they adapt to immediate context rather than stable psychological profiles. Without mechanisms to build and maintain persistent personality models, systems cannot achieve long-term consistency or psychological attunement required for therapeutic allegiance.

Deploying LLMs in multi-turn personality inference requires robust orchestration frameworks balancing flexibility with behavioral control. The PROMISE framework [7] enables dynamic prompt assembly through hierarchically nested states and explicit transition logic, providing structured control mechanisms for managing complex dialogue flows while maintaining prompt consistency.

## 2.3. AI Companions and Digital Interventions

Commercial AI companions and research prototypes demonstrate potential for conversational agents to reduce loneliness and provide mental health support [21–24]. Replika has engaged millions seeking emotional companionship; Woebot has shown effectiveness reducing depression and anxiety symptoms through structured CBT conversations; ElliQ demonstrates promise combating social isolation for older adults [22]. Systematic reviews indicate digital interventions produce modest but significant loneliness reductions (d = 0.2–0.5) [23,24].

However, most systems rely on fixed interaction strategies or limited personalization based on explicit user preferences. Broadbent et al. [22] note ElliQ uses rule-based personalization rather than dynamic personality adaptation, creating fundamental mismatch: while users' emotional states fluctuate and conversational patterns evolve, system interaction styles remain fixed, potentially limiting therapeutic rapport depth.

## 2.4. Personality-Aware Dialogue Systems

Research on personality-aware dialogue systems remains limited. Mairesse and Walker [6] demonstrated that adapting linguistic style to match user Big Five traits improves perceived naturalness and engagement. Zhou et al. [30] extended this to interview chatbots, showing automatic personality profiling can guide design decisions.

These systems typically rely on static personality inputs provided explicitly or inferred once at interaction start [6,30]. This approach has critical limitations: (1) requiring users to complete personality assessments before interaction creates friction and potential dropout, and (2) inability to adapt as conversational evidence accumulates.

## 2.5. Theoretical Foundations in Social Motivation

Computational approaches to personality-aware dialogue often lack cognitive-motivational theory explaining why personality traits should influence conversational behavior. The Zurich Model of Social Motivation [10,26–29] provides this foundation by linking Big Five traits to three fundamental cognitive-motivational systems: security (threat detection and safety maintenance), arousal (novelty-seeking and stimulation), and affiliation (social connection and cooperation).

The Zurich Model builds on earlier motivation theories [26,27] and Bischof's systems analysis [28,29], proposing that personality traits reflect stable individual differences in cognitive architecture governing motivational balance. Despite theoretical richness, the Zurich Model has not been widely operationalized for conversational AI.

## 2.6. Challenges in Evaluating LLM-Based Interventions

A persistent methodological challenge is evaluation validity. Traditional approaches rely on human expert ratings providing gold-standard assessments but are expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to scale [25]. Recent work explores LLM-based evaluation as a scalable alternative, demonstrating that structured LLM evaluation can correlate with human judgments. However, this raises concerns about circular validation: using AI to evaluate AI-generated outputs may introduce systematic biases [5].

## 2.7. Synthesis of Gaps and Study Contributions

Our review reveals three critical gaps addressed through a novel personality-adaptive framework:

1.  **Static vs. Dynamic Modeling Gap**: Existing systems rely on static personality profiles initialized before interaction.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We implement dynamic personality modeling as continuous trait inference, updating Big Five estimates based on linguistic cues throughout interaction.

2.  **Theoretical Gap**: Current approaches lack deep cognitive-motivational grounding, often mapping traits to surface-level linguistic style.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We operationalize this theoretical framework, explicitly mapping detected Big Five traits to behavioral adaptations across security, arousal, and affiliation domains.

3.  **Methodological Gap**: Orchestration frameworks like PROMISE [7] provide technical infrastructure but lack reproducible, theoretically grounded implementations integrating personality psychology with expert-validated evaluation.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We establish validated assessment rigor through hybrid evaluation combining LLM-based scalability with human expert validation (κ = 0.89), implemented within the PROMISE orchestration framework (Section 3.2).

**Positioning Relative to Prior Work**: While existing personality-aware systems [6,30] rely on static profile initialization and ad-hoc adaptation rules, our framework uniquely integrates: (1) dynamic turn-by-turn trait inference with cumulative evidence accumulation, (2) theory-driven regulation mapping traits to motivational domains, and (3) validated hybrid evaluation combining LLM-based scalability with human expert benchmarking (κ = 0.89).


# 3. Materials and Methods

This section presents simulation-based experimental methodology testing whether dynamic personality modeling, theoretically grounded regulation, and validated evaluation produce measurable improvements in conversational personalization. We employ a 2×2 factorial design comparing personality-adaptive (regulated) agents against non-adaptive (baseline) agents across two extreme personality profiles.

## 3.1. Overview and Research Objectives

### 3.1.1. Study Aims

This study addresses three empirical questions:

1. **Detection Accuracy**: Can stable personality traits be accurately inferred from conversational cues in real time within multi-turn interactions?

2. **Regulation Effectiveness**: Does theory-driven personality-adaptive regulation produce measurable improvements addressing personality-specific needs beyond generic responses?

3. **Evaluation Validity**: Do LLM-based evaluations align with independent human expert judgments, establishing a scalable yet reliable evaluation approach?

### 3.1.2. Experimental Design

We employed a 2×2 factorial design [31,32] comparing two assistant types (regulated vs. baseline) across two personality profiles (Type A vs. Type B), with 5 dialogue sessions per cell (20 sessions total; 120 dialogue turns). Equivalently, this can be viewed as 10 simulated personas (5 Type A, 5 Type B) each evaluated under two assistant conditions (regulated and baseline). These extreme configurations are intentionally used as boundary-condition personality profiles to verify detection and regulation fidelity under maximal signal conditions.

Regulated agents implemented dynamic personality detection and trait-aligned behavioral regulation based on the Zurich Model [10,26–29], updating personality inferences per-turn. Baseline agents provided emotionally supportive responses without personality-specific adaptation. Each agent was implemented as a separate GPT-4 instance (OpenAI API, model: gpt-4-0613, accessed June–August 2024) with customized system prompts.

The workflow encompassed four phases: (1) system configuration and personality profile implementation, (2) simulated dialogue generation following standardized protocols, (3) dual evaluation using LLM-based assessment and human expert validation, and (4) statistical analysis (Figure 1).

### 3.1.3. Sample Size Considerations

The 20 dialogue sessions (5 per cell; 10 per assistant type when aggregated across profiles) represent distinct GPT-4 instantiations with condition-specific system prompts, not human subjects. This sample size was selected for architectural validation purposes based on: (1) deterministic simulation with minimal stochastic variation, (2) computational cost constraints appropriate for proof-of-concept testing, and (3) focus on demonstrating implementation fidelity rather than estimating population-level effects [33].

Traditional power analysis is inappropriate because: (1) simulations are largely deterministic, (2) observed ceiling effects (zero variance) violate power analysis assumptions, and (3) the goal is technical validation, not inferential generalization.

Future human validation will require substantially larger samples (minimum n = 50–100 per condition) following clinical trial standards [35,36] to account for genuine individual variability, attrition, diverse personality distributions, spontaneous communication patterns, and real-world contextual factors absent from this simulation.

### 3.1.4. Ethics and Transparency

No human subjects were involved; all conversations were generated through GPT-4 instance interactions following predefined personality profiles. Because all data were synthetic, non-identifiable, and generated solely for technical validation, formal ethics approval was not required [37].

Future human subject studies will require full IRB approval, comprehensive informed consent explaining AI personality profiling and data usage, real-time safety monitoring with distress escalation pathways, and regular mental health professional oversight [38,39].

## 3.2. Personality-Adaptive System Architecture

Our framework implements a modular pipeline A = (D, R, E) consisting of a Detection Module (D) for real-time OCEAN trait inference, a Regulation Module (R) for theory-aligned behavior adaptation, and an Evaluation Module (E) for quality assessment (Figure 2).

The architecture is built upon the PROMISE framework [7], which provides model-driven prompt orchestration through hierarchical state machine modeling. PROMISE serves as a reproducibility scaffold, ensuring deterministic prompt orchestration and traceable state transitions across experimental conditions. Our implementation extends PROMISE with personality-aware states and transitions, using Java 17 for state machine logic and GPT-4 API (model: gpt-4-0613) for natural language understanding and generation.

### 3.2.1. Design Principles

The architecture follows three core principles [40,41]:

(i) **Separation of Concerns**: Independent modules with well-defined interfaces enable parallel development and isolated testing. Modules communicate through standardized data structures (JSON) containing personality vectors, regulation instructions, and evaluation scores.

(ii) **Scalability**: New personality dimensions or regulation strategies can be added without modifying existing components. Trait-to-regulation mapping is externalized as configuration files.

(iii) **Traceability and Reproducibility**: All detection decisions, regulation instructions, and evaluation scores are logged with timestamps and dialogue turn identifiers. PROMISE's declarative state modeling ensures all behavioral adaptations are traceable to explicit state transitions.

### 3.2.2. Data Flow Pipeline

The system processes messages through a sequential pipeline (Figure 3): (1) User input and dialogue history capture, (2) Personality detection analyzes linguistic cues to infer OCEAN traits, (3) Trait-to-regulation mapping translates personality into behavioral adaptation instructions, (4) Prompt assembly combines regulation with dialogue context, (5) GPT-4 generates personality-adaptive response, (6) Evaluation assesses response quality, and (7) Outputs are logged.

## 3.3. Personality Detection Module

The Detection Module performs real-time Big Five trait inference by continuously monitoring and updating the user's OCEAN personality profile, incrementally adjusting estimation after each user input.

### 3.3.1. Personality Representation Framework

We employ the Five-Factor Model (FFM) [3,9] as personality representation P = (O, C, E, A, N) → {−1, 0, +1}?, capturing Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism. This discrete three-level encoding (+1 = high, 0 = neutral/insufficient evidence, −1 = low) enables tractable real-time inference while preserving sufficient granularity [42,44].

In this simulation, the personality vector functions as a reference target configuration for controlled evaluation rather than as psychometric ground truth derived from validated personality inventories. Detection accuracy denotes agreement with the pre-specified simulated profile, not validated personality assessment in human subjects.

| Trait | +1 (High) | −1 (Low) |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Openness | Curious, imaginative, open to novelty | Prefers routine, resistant to new ideas |
| Conscientiousness | Organized, disciplined, structured | Disorganized, impulsive, spontaneous |
| Extraversion | Outgoing, energetic, assertive | Reserved, quiet, withdrawn |
| Agreeableness | Cooperative, empathetic, friendly | Critical, skeptical, confrontational |
| Neuroticism* | Calm, emotionally stable, resilient | Anxious, emotionally sensitive, insecure |

*Note: Neuroticism is inverse-coded: +1 denotes emotional stability (low neuroticism), and −1 denotes emotional vulnerability (high neuroticism).

**Table 1.** Big Five personality trait operationalization.

### 3.3.2. Detection Algorithm

After every user input, the system recalculates the Big Five profile as a five-dimensional vector (O, C, E, A, N). The detection module uses a cumulative approach similar to Bayesian belief updating [45], progressively refining assessments based on entire dialogue history. Traits remain neutral (0) until sufficient evidence supports assigning a value, reducing premature classification [46].

![](complete_submission/Figure_04_Detection_Process.png)

### 3.3.3. PROMISE Framework Integration

The detection module is implemented using PROMISE [7] (see Section 3.2 for framework details).

**State Machine Architecture**: The detection module organizes as a hierarchical state machine with three layers: (1) Global Conversation State tracking dialogue progression, (2) Trait Detection States for each Big Five dimension operating in parallel, and (3) Confidence Assessment Substates managing evidence accumulation.

**Dynamic Prompt Orchestration**: PROMISE's dynamic prompt assembly based on state transitions enables context-sensitive personality detection [7]. As conversations progress through states, the system dynamically adjusts detection prompts by incorporating cumulative dialogue history, previously detected traits, and confidence scores.

**State Transitions and Actuators**: Personality vector updates trigger state transitions via PROMISE's actuator mechanism [7]. When sufficient evidence accumulates for a trait, the trait detection substate transitions from Neutral to Positive/Negative, automatically propagating changes to the regulation module.

### 3.3.4. Technical Implementation

The detection module is developed as a modular Java component (Java 17, OpenJDK) integrated into PROMISE's execution engine. Each Big Five trait operates within an independent state machine with well-defined input (user utterance, dialogue history) and output (trait score ∈ {−1, 0, +1}, confidence ∈ [0, 1]) interfaces. The system leverages GPT-4 instances (model: gpt-4-0613) for semantic understanding through trait-specific prompts encoding linguistic patterns from personality psychology literature [48-52].

Trait-specific detection prompts are instantiated as PROMISE templates: Openness detection focuses on vocabulary complexity, abstract thinking, novelty-seeking [48]; Conscientiousness identifies planning language, organizational patterns, goal-orientation [49]; Extraversion captures social engagement, energy level, assertiveness [50]; Agreeableness recognizes cooperative language, empathy, relational orientation [51]; Neuroticism analyzes emotional stability, anxiety markers, stress-related language [52].

### 3.3.5. Processing Pipeline

The detection pipeline executes eight steps orchestrated by PROMISE: (1) Message Processing: User input triggers Detection_Active state; input is preprocessed and combined with dialogue history. (2) Prompt Assembly: Instantiates trait-specific prompts; five trait detectors operate in parallel. (3) LLM Inference: GPT-4 API calls via PROMISE's managed interface. (4) Result Parsing: Extracts scores (-1, 0, +1) and confidence indicators. (5) Confidence Assessment: Evaluates detection confidence. (6) State Update: Updates personality vector P; trait-level state machines transition based on confidence thresholds (τ = 0.7). (7) Actuator Triggering: State changes propagate updates to regulation module. (8) Downstream Transmission: Transmits updated vector and confidence scores.

Average processing time: 2.3 ± 0.8 seconds (measured across 120 turns), with parallel trait detection reducing latency by ~60% versus sequential processing.

## 3.4. Behavior Regulation Module

The Regulation Module translates psychological information from continuously inferred personality traits into theoretically grounded behavioral adaptations.

### 3.4.1. Purpose and Theoretical Foundation

The module dynamically modifies assistant interactions based on continuously updated personality profiles, applying the Zurich Model of Social Motivation [10,26–29] (introduced in Section 2.5) to adapt tone, structure, and interpersonal stance across three motivational domains: Security, Arousal, and Affiliation.

### 3.4.2. Trait-to-Domain Mapping

Our regulation strategy maps OCEAN traits to the three motivational domains [10]: Security (Neuroticism → emotional stability/vulnerability responses), Arousal (Openness and Extraversion → novelty/stimulation regulation), and Affiliation (Agreeableness → social connection strategies).

### 3.4.3. Dynamic Prompt Construction

After each personality update, the module retrieves relevant behavior prompts for all non-neutral traits and concatenates them into a single integrated regulation instruction. For example, profile (−1, +1, −1, −1, −1) prompts the assistant to: focus on familiar topics (Low Openness), provide organized guidance (High Conscientiousness), adopt calm reflective style (Low Extraversion), use neutral stance (Low Agreeableness), and offer extra comfort (High Neuroticism).

When multiple traits suggest conflicting behaviors, the system employs harmonization strategies prioritizing emotional safety (Security domain) over stimulation (Arousal domain) following clinical guidelines [53].

## 3.5. Experimental Materials and Procedure

### 3.5.1. Personality Profile Simulation

We implemented two boundary-condition personality profiles representing extremes of human personality variation [42,44,55]. Type A (High-functioning) embodied extreme positive traits (personality vector: +1, +1, +1, +1, +1). Type B (Vulnerable) represented opposite extremes (−1, −1, −1, −1, −1).

These extreme configurations were deliberately selected to maximize signal for detection and regulation mechanisms. While not representative of typical distributions [3], such profiles offer superior diagnostic value for validating algorithms before testing with moderate profiles [55]. 

Detection and regulation performance are evaluated against these synthetic reference profiles as ground truth within the simulation environment. Profiles were encoded into GPT-4 system prompts consistently exhibiting target trait levels across turns.

### 3.5.2. Agent Configuration

Twenty dialogue sessions (assistant-condition instantiations) were generated across four 2×2 cells: Regulated-TypeA (n=5), Regulated-TypeB (n=5), Baseline-TypeA (n=5), Baseline-TypeB (n=5). Regulated assistants received system prompts incorporating dynamic detection modules and trait-aligned regulation instructions, with inferences updating per-turn. Baseline assistants received static supportive response prompts without personality-specific adaptation. Each assistant instantiation used a separate GPT-4 call configuration (model: gpt-4-0613, temperature=0.7, max_tokens=500).

### 3.5.3. Dialogue Structure and Data Collection

For each personality type (A and B), we generated 5 regulated and 5 baseline dialogue sessions. Each session consisted of 6 user–assistant exchanges, resulting in 120 dialogue turns total (2 profiles × 2 assistant types × 5 sessions × 6 turns). All conversations followed a standardized six-turn structure balancing sufficient interaction depth for valid personality detection with manageable evaluation complexity.

Turn sequence: (1) Assistant opening offering support, (2) User response exhibiting personality-congruent patterns, (3) Assistant adaptive/generic response, (4) User follow-up elaborating concerns, (5) Assistant final adaptive response, (6) User concluding statement.

User messages were generated by separate GPT-4 instances (model: gpt-4-0613) instantiated with personality profile prompts ensuring consistent trait expression. Conversations were exported into structured Evaluation Matrices (Excel format). Data collection occurred over 4 weeks (June–July 2024).

## 3.6. Evaluation Procedure

### 3.6.1. Evaluation Criteria and Scoring Framework

The evaluation measured effectiveness of personality-regulated assistant interactions versus baselines. Regulated assistants were evaluated across five criteria: Detection Accuracy, Regulation Effectiveness, Emotional Tone Appropriate, Relevance & Coherence, and Personality Needs Addressed.

Baseline assistants were assessed on three criteria only (Emotional Tone, Relevance & Coherence, Personality Needs), as detection and regulation were not implemented. All criteria used a trinary scoring scale (Yes = 2, Not Sure = 1, No = 0) [56].

![](complete_submission/Figure_07_Evaluation_Framework.png)

### 3.6.2. LLM-Based Evaluator System

A specialized Evaluator GPT-4 instance was developed using custom-designed prompts for each criterion, following the "LLM-as-judge" paradigm [57,58]. The evaluator assessed responses objectively by analyzing conversations one row at a time. Measures to mitigate evaluator bias: evaluations conducted separately for regulated and baseline assistants; comprehensive context provided including full dialogue history and personality profile; evaluator informed that detection accuracy improves incrementally; temperature set to 0.3 to reduce stochastic variation.

### 3.6.3. Human Expert Validation Protocol

To address "AI-evaluating-AI" concerns, we implemented human-expert validation [59,60]. Two independent human experts (Expert 1: Clinical Psychology with 14 years experience; Expert 2: AI/Computer Science in healthcare systems with 10 years experience) independently evaluated the full dataset of 120 dialogue turns (i.e., all 20 dialogue sessions × 6 turns), covering all four 2×2 cells (Regulated-TypeA, Regulated-TypeB, Baseline-TypeA, Baseline-TypeB).

Experts used the identical evaluation matrix and trinary scoring scale. All identifying information was removed; dialogues were presented in random sequence; and raters had no access to Evaluator GPT assessments or each other's scores until data collection was complete.

### 3.6.4. Validation Results and Agreement Analysis

Inter-rater reliability between human experts yielded strong agreement (Krippendorff's α = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.76–0.88), exceeding the α > 0.80 threshold for acceptable agreement [11]. Agreement between Evaluator GPT and consensus human ratings was excellent (Cohen's κ = 0.89, 95% CI: 0.84–0.94).

Criterion-specific analysis revealed consistent agreement: Detection Accuracy (κ = 0.92), Regulation Effectiveness (κ = 0.88), Emotional Tone (κ = 0.86), Relevance & Coherence (κ = 0.91), and Personality Needs (κ = 0.87). This strong alignment demonstrates that observed performance differences are not artifacts of AI-rating-AI bias but reflect genuine quality distinctions detectable by human experts.

## 3.7. Statistical Analysis

### 3.7.1. Primary and Secondary Outcomes

Primary outcome was performance on the "Personality Needs Addressed" criterion, measured on a trinary scale (Yes = 2, Not Sure = 1, No = 0). Secondary outcomes included Detection Accuracy, Regulation Effectiveness, Emotional Tone Appropriateness, and Relevance & Coherence. Metrics were aggregated at the dialogue-session level (n = 20 total sessions; n = 10 per assistant type; n = 5 per 2×2 cell) [61].

### 3.7.2. Effect Size Calculation

We calculated Cohen's d for between-group comparisons using pooled standard deviation:

$$d = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{\sqrt{\frac{SD_1^2 + SD_2^2}{2}}}$$

where M1 and M2 represent mean scores for regulated and baseline conditions. Effect sizes were interpreted using conventional thresholds: small (d = 0.2), medium (d = 0.5), large (d = 0.8) [62]. Confidence intervals (95%) were calculated using bias-corrected bootstrap (10,000 iterations) in R [63].

### 3.7.3. Statistical Testing

Between-group differences were evaluated using two-tailed independent samples t-tests for normally distributed metrics and Mann-Whitney U tests for non-normally distributed metrics. Normality was assessed using Shapiro-Wilk tests (α = 0.05). Statistical significance was set at α = 0.05. Inferential statistics are reported for comparability with prior conversational AI studies and should not be interpreted as evidence of population-level effects [64].

### 3.7.4. Inter-Rater Reliability

Agreement between human expert raters was quantified using Krippendorff's α [11]. Agreement between LLM evaluator and consensus human ratings was quantified using Cohen's κ [65]. Confidence intervals (95%) were calculated using bias-corrected bootstrap (10,000 iterations). Reliability thresholds: α or κ > 0.80 indicates acceptable agreement; > 0.90 indicates excellent agreement.

### 3.7.6. Software and Computational Environment

All statistical analyses were conducted in R version 4.3.1 using the effsize package (v0.8.1) for Cohen's d, irr package (v0.84.1) for inter-rater reliability, and boot package (v1.3-28) for bootstrap confidence intervals. Visualizations used ggplot2 (v3.4.2).

**Critical Interpretation Note**: The observed effect size (d = 4.58) is primarily a mathematical artifact of the experimental design rather than evidence of clinical effectiveness. **What This Effect Size Demonstrates**: Cohen's d = 4.58 confirms that the two experimental conditions produce dramatically different outputs when tested in simulation, validating that the architecture functions differently as designed.

**What This Effect Size Does NOT Demonstrate**: This metric does not indicate the system would achieve similar performance with real human users, does not predict clinical effectiveness, and does not generalize beyond the controlled simulation environment. Human conversations involve ambiguous language, mixed personality profiles, spontaneous communication, and contextual complexity entirely absent from this controlled test.

# 5. Discussion

## 5.1. Principal Findings

This simulation study examined a framework combining Big Five detection with theory-driven regulation. As detailed in Section 4, the system achieved complete technical accuracy, yielding large improvements in personality-specific need fulfillment (d = 4.58) without compromising basic conversational quality. This represents a methodological validation—demonstrating architectural feasibility—rather than evidence of clinical performance.

## 5.2. Interpretation of Perfect Performance

Perfect performance observed for personality detection and regulation should be interpreted cautiously. The study was intentionally designed to create favorable conditions: extreme personality profiles, pre-scripted user utterances, short dialogues, and clearly defined evaluation criteria.

Rather than indicating perfect performance is attainable in practice, these results should be viewed as an upper bound on what the current architecture can achieve in highly controlled settings. In real-world use, personality traits are more moderate and mixed, users express themselves in varied and ambiguous ways, and conversational histories are longer and less predictable. Under such conditions, performance would likely degrade and variability increase.

## 5.3. Comparison with Existing Literature

Our findings align with prior work suggesting personality-aware systems can improve interaction quality versus generic approaches [6,30]. Unlike previous systems that relied on static profiles or ad-hoc rules, our framework implements continuous trait inference with principled regulation grounded in the theoretical framework described in Section 3.4.

Methodologically, the validated LLM-based evaluator (κ = 0.89 vs. human experts) addresses "AI-evaluating-AI" concerns and suggests structured automated assessment can approximate expert judgment for conversational quality criteria.

## 5.4. Implications for Research and Practice

From a research perspective, results indicate trait-based regulation is a promising direction for conversational AI in mental health-adjacent applications. Architectures that explicitly model stable traits may help systems maintain consistent, user-congruent styles over time, potentially improving engagement and user satisfaction. The modular design facilitates independent improvement and targeted experimentation with each component.

For practice, the study does not provide evidence that this approach is ready for clinical use. Nonetheless, it highlights design principles relevant for future systems supporting mental health professionals: explicit representation of personality information, transparent mappings from traits to regulation strategies, logging of adaptation decisions, and mechanisms for human oversight.

## 5.5. Toward Future Clinical Research

**Critical Caveat**: This simulation study provides no evidence that this approach is ready for, or would be effective in, clinical applications. Translation to clinical practice would require addressing fundamental gaps:

- **Validated Clinical Outcomes**: Effect sizes observed here (d = 4.58) reflect AI-judged conversation quality in controlled simulation, not patient-reported outcomes, symptom reduction, therapeutic alliance, or sustained engagement measured using validated instruments.

- **Safety Infrastructure**: Any healthcare application would require comprehensive safety protocols including distress detection, crisis escalation pathways, adverse event monitoring, and validation that personality profiling does not introduce bias or harm.

- **Extensive Human Validation**: Multi-site trials with diverse populations, longitudinal tracking, real-world effectiveness evaluation, and rigorous ethical and regulatory review are mandatory prerequisites.

These findings establish architectural feasibility, not clinical readiness.

## 5.6. Limitations and Future Work

This study has several important limitations. All interactions were simulated between predefined personality profiles and GPT-4-based agents; no human users participated, and results cannot be generalized to patients or clinical settings. Profiles were restricted to extreme configurations (all traits high vs. all traits low), not reflecting typical personality distributions. Dialogues were short (six turns per agent), unable to capture long-term dynamics. Both system and evaluator rely on the same LLM family, potentially introducing shared biases despite strong human-AI agreement. The work focused on English-language text and Western communication norms; cultural and linguistic generalizability remains unknown.

Future research should progressively relax these simplifying assumptions. Immediate priorities include testing with moderate and mixed personality profiles, longer and more naturalistic conversations, and more diverse linguistic and cultural contexts. Human subject studies are essential to evaluate whether personality adaptation improves user-reported outcomes and to identify potential harms or failure modes. Methodological work should refine multimodal personality detection, uncertainty estimation for personality inferences, and safeguards for detecting when the system operates outside competence range.


# 6. Conclusions

This simulation study demonstrates that integrating real-time Big Five personality detection with theory-driven behavioral regulation is architecturally feasible using current LLM technology. As reported in Section 4, the system achieved complete technical accuracy and produced a selective enhancement pattern—dramatically improving personality-specific need fulfillment while maintaining baseline-equivalent conversational quality—thereby confirming theoretical predictions.

The validated hybrid evaluation approach (LLM-based ratings benchmarked against human experts, κ = 0.89) offers a reproducible and scalable methodology for future conversational AI research.

These results represent an upper bound; real-world deployment will require extensive human validation as outlined in Section 5.5–5.6. Future priorities include human trials with diverse populations and moderate personality profiles, longitudinal outcome tracking, and comprehensive bias audits before clinical piloting.

**Broader Impact**: This work provides a validated methodological template (detection → regulation → evaluation) adaptable to different personality theories or clinical domains, opening pathways toward personalized digital mental health interventions. However, translation to clinical utility requires sustained interdisciplinary collaboration to ensure safety, effectiveness, equity, and alignment with human values.
