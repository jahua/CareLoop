# 3. Materials and Methods
Recent advances in affective computing and large language model–based conversational systems have demonstrated that emotionally responsive dialogue and context-aware language generation are technically feasible in mental health–adjacent applications. However, existing approaches largely treat personalization as a function of transient emotional states or surface-level contextual cues, rather than as an adaptive process grounded in stable individual differences. As a result, most systems lack mechanisms for dynamically modeling personality, translating personality information into theoretically informed behavior regulation, and evaluating personalization effects with sufficient methodological rigor.

To address these limitations, this study adopts a controlled simulation-based methodology designed to isolate the contribution of personality-adaptive mechanisms in conversational AI. The proposed system integrates real-time Big Five personality trait inference with behavior regulation grounded in the Zurich Model of Social Motivation, allowing the assistant to adapt its interaction style across dialogue turns in response to stable personality characteristics. To distinguish the effects of personality-driven adaptation from general conversational competence, the regulated system is compared against a non-adaptive baseline that provides emotionally supportive but personality-agnostic responses. All interactions employ standardized simulated personas and a fixed dialogue structure to ensure experimental control and reproducibility.

The methodological framework is organized around three core objectives. First, we examine whether stable personality traits can be accurately inferred from conversational cues in real time within multi-turn interactions. Second, we evaluate whether theory-driven personality-adaptive regulation produces measurable improvements in addressing personality-specific user needs beyond generic supportive responses. Third, we assess the validity of an LLM-based evaluation framework by benchmarking its judgments against independent human expert ratings, thereby establishing a scalable yet reliable approach to evaluating conversational quality and personalization. Together, these objectives define a coherent methodological pathway for evaluating personality-adaptive conversational AI and provide a foundation for subsequent empirical extensions beyond simulation-based research.

## 3.1. Study Design

This study evaluates a personality-adaptive conversational AI system under controlled simulation conditions. The system integrates real-time Big Five personality inference with behavior regulation grounded in the Zurich Model of Social Motivation, enabling continuous updating of personality representation and adaptive interaction. We employed a **2�2 factorial design** comparing two assistant types (regulated vs. baseline) across two personality profiles (Type A vs. Type B), with 5 independent agent instances per condition (20 total agents, 120 dialogue turns). Baseline agents served as controls, providing emotionally supportive responses without personality adaptation.

**Sample Size Justification**: While modest for traditional human subject research, the sample is justified by: (1) exceptionally large observed effect sizes (Cohen's d = 4.58), (2) near-zero variance in regulated condition indicating ceiling effects, (3) deterministic simulation design reducing random variation, and (4) computational constraints appropriate for early-stage proof-of-concept research. Post-hoc power analysis confirms power > 0.99 at n = 10 per group. Future human subject studies will require substantially larger samples (minimum n = 50-100 per condition).

**Research Objectives**: (1) examine whether stable personality traits can be accurately inferred from conversational cues in real time, (2) assess whether personality-adaptive regulation improves addressing personality-specific user needs beyond generic responses, and (3) validate LLM-based assessment framework against independent human expert evaluations.

**Ethics Statement**: No human subjects were involved; all conversations were simulated interactions with predefined personality profiles. Because data was synthetic and non-identifiable, formal ethics approval was not required. Future human subject studies will require full IRB approval, informed consent protocols, and comprehensive safety monitoring.

## 3.3. Personality Detection Module

**Purpose and Foundation**: The Personality Detection Module dynamically monitors and updates the user's Big Five (OCEAN) personality profile throughout the conversation, incrementally adjusting personality estimation after each user input. We employ the Five-Factor Model as personality representation P = (O, C, E, A, N) ? {?1, 0, +1}?, capturing Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism. This discrete encoding enables tractable real-time inference while preserving sufficient granularity for research applications.

**Detection Logic**: Each user�chatbot dialogue turn is parsed individually and cumulatively. After every user input, the system recalculates the Big Five personality profile as a five-dimensional vector (O, C, E, A, N). Trait values are assigned as +1 (high trait expression), ?1 (low trait expression), or 0 (neutral or insufficient evidence). The detection module uses a cumulative approach, progressively updating personality assessments based on entire dialogue history. Traits remain neutral (0) until sufficient dialogue evidence clearly supports assigning a positive or negative value, reducing premature trait classification.

## 3.4. Behavior Regulation Module

**Purpose**: The Behavior Regulation Module dynamically modifies chatbot interactions based on the user's continuously updated personality profile. Grounded in the Zurich Model of Social Motivation, this module adapts the assistant's tone, structure, and interpersonal stance to align with the user's Big Five personality traits, enhancing engagement, reducing emotional friction, and creating naturally supportive conversations.

**Zurich Model Integration**: Our regulation strategy maps OCEAN traits to Zurich Model motivational domains [10]: **Security Domain** (Neuroticism ? emotional stability/vulnerability responses through reassurance or comfort provision), **Arousal Domain** (Openness and Extraversion ? novelty/stimulation regulation by encouraging or tempering novelty and social energy), **Affiliation Domain** (Agreeableness ? social connection/cooperation strategies through adjusting warmth and relational proximity).

When multiple traits suggest potentially conflicting behaviors, the system employs harmonization strategies to create coherent responses.

## 3.5. Experimental Protocol

**Personality Profile Simulation**: We implemented two extreme personality profiles representing boundary conditions of human personality variation. Type A (High-functioning) embodied extreme conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and emotional stability combined with high openness. Type B (Vulnerable) represented opposite extremes. These extreme profiles were deliberately selected to maximize signal for personality detection and regulation mechanisms, providing clear test cases for system capability assessment in controlled conditions. Personality profiles were encoded into conversational prompts that consistently exhibited target trait levels across all dialogue turns.

**Agent Configuration**: Our experimental design employed 20 distinct agent instances deployed across two personality types, with randomized assignment to personality conditions to minimize initialization bias. Regulated agents received system prompts incorporating dynamic personality detection and trait-aligned behavioral regulation, updating on a per-turn basis. Baseline agents received static supportive response prompts without personality-specific adaptation, serving as essential control conditions. Each agent was implemented as a separate GPT-4 instance with customized system prompts.

**Dialogue Structure**: For each personality type, 5 regulated and 5 baseline bots engaged in 6 user-assistant exchanges (turns), resulting in 120 dialogue turns total. All conversations followed a standardized six-turn structure balancing sufficient interaction depth for valid personality detection while maintaining manageable evaluation complexity. Turn sequence: (1) Assistant opening statement, (2) User response exhibiting personality-congruent communication, (3) Assistant adaptive/generic response, (4) User follow-up, (5) Assistant final adaptive response, (6) User concluding statement. This structure enabled personality inference across varying interaction depths�early turns for initial detection, middle turns for consistency, late turns for sustained adaptation. Conversations were systematically exported into a structured Evaluation Matrix capturing user messages, assistant replies, detected personality vectors (regulated only), applied regulation prompts (regulated only), and evaluation metrics, with consistent naming convention ([Personality]-[Type]-[Bot Number]).

## 3.6. Evaluation Framework

**Evaluation Criteria**: The evaluation methodology systematically measured effectiveness of behavior-regulated chatbot interactions compared to baseline counterparts. Regulated assistants were evaluated across five criteria: **Detection Accuracy** (whether detected OCEAN vector accurately reflects user's expressed behavior), **Regulation Effectiveness** (whether personality-specific behavior strategies are correctly applied based on detected traits), **Emotional Tone Appropriate** (whether emotional tone matches user's current state and personality traits), **Relevance & Coherence** (whether response is contextually relevant and logically coherent), and **Personality Needs Addressed** (whether specific emotional and interactional needs derived from user's personality profile are effectively addressed). Baseline assistants were assessed on three criteria only (Emotional Tone, Relevance & Coherence, Personality Needs). All criteria used a trinary scoring scale (Yes = 2, Not Sure = 1, No = 0).

**Evaluator System**: A specialized Evaluator GPT was developed using custom-designed prompts tailored for evaluation criteria. This GPT-based evaluator assessed assistant responses objectively, analyzing conversations one row at a time without referencing previous evaluations, explicitly restating dialogue content for transparency. Several rigorous measures mitigated evaluator bias: evaluations conducted separately for regulated and baseline assistants to avoid cross-influence, comprehensive context provided per interaction pair, evaluator informed that personality detection accuracy improved incrementally, and custom Excel formulas automated aggregation ensuring transparency and replicability.

**Human-Expert Validation**: To address "AI-evaluating-AI" concerns, we implemented rigorous human-expert validation. Two PhD-level in-house research experts (one Clinical Psychology with 14 years experience in mental health interventions, one Computer Science with 10 years experience in AI-based healthcare systems) independently evaluated a stratified random sample of 30 dialogue turns (25% of dataset). Stratification ensured balanced representation across conditions (50% regulated/baseline), personality types (50% Type A/B), and turn positions (33% early/mid/late). Experts employed identical evaluation matrix and trinary scoring scale. All identifying information was removed; dialogues presented in random sequence; raters had no access to Evaluator GPT assessments or each other's scores until data collection complete.

**Validation Results**: Inter-rater reliability between two experts yielded strong agreement (Krippendorff's ? = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.76-0.88), exceeding ? > 0.80 threshold for acceptable clinical research agreement. Agreement between Evaluator GPT and consensus human ratings (Cohen's ? = 0.89, 95% CI: 0.84-0.94) indicated excellent alignment with independent expert judgment. Criterion-specific analysis revealed consistent agreement: Detection Accuracy (? = 0.92), Regulation Effectiveness (? = 0.88), Emotional Tone (? = 0.86), Relevance & Coherence (? = 0.91), and Personality Needs (? = 0.87). This strong alignment demonstrates that observed performance differences are not artifacts of AI-rating-AI bias but reflect genuine quality distinctions detectable by human experts.
