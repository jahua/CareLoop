## Abstract
Abstract: Large language model agents are increasingly deployed to expand access to digital emotion support and mental health care by offering timely, low-cost conversational assistance. Yet, most current systems rely on generic communication strategies that overlook stable individual differences in personality, limiting their capacity to deliver personalized interventions aligned with personal psychological coping mechanisms. This study maps how personality-adaptive mechanisms integrate real-time Big Five personality trait detection with theoretically grounded regulation strategies based on the Zurich Model of Social Motivation.

Methods: We conducted a controlled study comparing a personality-adaptive system against a non-adaptive baseline across two extreme Big Five profiles: one very high and one very low on openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and emotional stability under contrasting psychological conditions. The adaptive system integrated GPT-4-based modules for personality detection, regulation mapping, and evaluation. Conversational quality and personality alignment were evaluated by an LLM judge with responses rated by psychological experts on detection accuracy, regulation effectiveness, emotional tone, relevance and personality-specific emotional needs. 

Results: Across the simulated conditions, the regulated system achieved near-perfect performance in personality detection and regulation effectiveness. The personality-adaptive agents demonstrated very large improvements in addressing personality-specific needs compared to baseline (M = 2.00 vs. 0.17 on a 0–2 scale; Cohen's d = 4.58, p < 0.001), representing a >90% improvement in personalization, while maintaining comparable basic conversational quality (effect sizes d < 0.3, p > 0.05). Agreement between AI evaluator and human experts was excellent (Cohen's κ = 0.89, 95% CI: 0.84–0.94), validating the evaluation approach. These results establish proof-of-concept under idealized conditions but do not constitute evidence for real-world effectiveness. Reported benefits included enhanced personalization, improved alignment with individual psychological needs, and validated evaluation methodology. However, recurring challenges such as reliance on extreme simulated profiles, pre-scripted interactions, brief conversations, and limited generalizability highlight the need for ethical design and human oversight. 

Conclusion: By introducing a personality-adaptive framework, this simulation study offers a comprehensive overview of current applications and future directions in AI-augmented emotional support. It aims to guide researchers, clinicians, and policymakers in developing safe, effective, and equitable personality-adaptive digital mental health interventions.

**Keywords:** digital mental health; conversational agents; large language models; personality detection; Big Five; Zurich Model; adaptive interaction; emotional support; simulation study; GPT-4


# 1. Introduction

Despite major advances in economic and technological development, mental health and social well-being remain pressing global public health challenges. Loneliness and social isolation among older adults are particularly persistent concerns, contributing to morbidity and mortality risks comparable to obesity and approaching those of heavy smoking [1]. Demographic shifts such as population aging, dispersed families, bereavement, and declining community engagement further limit opportunities for meaningful social contact [2]. In Switzerland, as in many high-income contexts, a growing proportion of adults aged 75 and older live alone and are especially vulnerable to isolation [3]. These trends have intensified interest in scalable digital approaches that complement traditional services while providing accessible, timely social and emotional support.

Within this landscape, digital mental health tools and conversational assistants are being explored as mechanisms for increasing access to supportive interactions. However, most existing systems still rely on rule-based communication strategies that offer limited flexibility in addressing diverse user needs [4]. These systems operate with predefined scripts and fixed response templates, making their effectiveness variable and their supportive value constrained.

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have enabled more flexible, context-aware conversational AI applications capable of generating simulated empathy and emotionally relevant responses [8,9]. However, their supportive behaviors remain largely generic and one-size-fits-all, as they lack mechanisms for dynamically adapting to stable user characteristics. Although natural language processing techniques that parse user input, perform sentiment analysis, and incorporate recalled preferences are widely used, they typically provide only shallow personalization and often still rely on static assessments collected before interaction, rather than dynamically modeling and adapting to stable user traits ground in psychologocal principle.

To address these gaps, we developed a personality-adaptive conversational framework that integrates (i) real-time Big Five personality traits detection, (ii) behavior regulation grounded in the Zurich Model of Social Motivation, and (iii) a structured evaluation pipeline using automated LLM-based evaluation. The framework is implemented using GPT-4–based agents performs real-time detection of Big Five traits that continuously infers and updates stable trait estimates based on linguistic cues throughout conversation and response theory-guided behavioral adaptation.

The objective of this study is to determine whether real-time personality-adaptive behavior regulation, grounded in continuous inference of user traits from the Big Five personality framework from psychology, that can enhance conversational quality, emotional alignment, and psychological attunement in chatbot–user interactions. Specifically, we implement and evaluate a real-time detection and regulation loop in which an adaptive AI assistant infers personality across multiple dialogue turns and adjusts its tone and behavioral style accordingly, and compare its effectiveness against static, non-adaptive baselines in delivering emotionally supportive interactions. moving beyond preliminary feasibility to workflow integration and reproducible we integrate this pipline into PROMISE, a framework building for interacte with LLM applications by orchestrate dynamic prompts across hierarchical states with attached conditions and actions and predictable language-model behavior in complex interactions. This design ensures precise control over interaction parameters, clear quantification of personalization effects, and a fully reproducible testbed for personality-adaptive conversational agents. It offers a solid methodological foundation to guide researchers, clinicians, and policymakers in developing safe, effective, and equitable AI-augmented mental health interventions.


# 2. Related Work
The development of personality-adaptive conversational AI represents a convergence of AI-driven digital interventions, including natural language processing, machine learning, and large language models, with personality psychology and affective computing. These systems aim to not only process information but understand context, adapt to individual differences, and support personalized mental health care through personality-adaptive architectures. Within this paradigm, personality-adaptive systems represent a shift from reactive information processing toward dynamic personality modeling that captures stable individual differences in how humans perceive, process, and respond to emotional and social information.

This section traces the evolution from generic therapeutic support systems toward personality-adaptive AI architectures, building upon three interconnected research streams: (1) affective computing and emotion-aware systems, (2) conversational AI architectures including transformer-based LLMs, and (3) personality psychology theories that explain individual differences in emotional processing. We systematically review the state of the art in each domain before synthesizing critical gaps that motivate our investigation.

## 2.1. Affective Computing and Emotion Recognition

Early affective computing research established foundational techniques for recognizing emotional states from text, speech, and facial expressions [12,13]. Initial approaches relied heavily on **categorical emotion models** (e.g., happiness, sadness) before evolving toward **dimensional representations** that model affect distinct valence and arousal components. While these systems demonstrated the feasibility of detecting emotional signals, they face persistent challenges with generalization across individuals and contexts, often struggling with the subtle, mixed emotional expressions common in real-world interactions [12,13].

From a systems perspective, such signal-driven detection implements *shallow affective modeling*—recognizing surface-level emotional signals without accounting for the **cognitive and motivational context**. A critical limitation is the emphasis on *transient emotional states* rather than *stable personality traits*. Most work focuses on detecting *what* users are currently feeling (e.g., anxiety) to generate empathetic responses, but overlooks *who* the user is—specifically how stable personality differences shape emotional processing and regulation needs [6]. This reliance on transient state detection leaves a gap in understanding the stable architectural differences that determine effective support strategies.

## 2.2. Large Language Models in Mental Health

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has transformed conversational AI capabilities, positioning them as powerful conversational AI engines capable of flexible reasoning, context integration, and natural language understanding [8,15,16]. These transformer-based generative models with billions of parameters (e.g., GPT-3.5/4) implement key cognitive functions—semantic memory, analogical reasoning, theory of mind inference—through learned statistical patterns over vast text corpora, enabling free-text generation, contextual memory, and zero-shot reasoning. Recent systems leverage these capabilities for therapeutic support through various architectural approaches: dialogue state tracking to maintain conversation context [15], cascaded neural networks to enhance empathy generation [16], and multimodal integration combining text, audio, and physiological signals [17,19,20].

Despite these advances, current LLM-based therapeutic support systems frequently exhibit a "one-size-fits-all" limitation. While they generate contextually appropriate empathetic responses, they typically adapt to the immediate context rather than the user's stable psychological profile. Without mechanisms to build and maintain persistent personality models, these systems cannot maintain the long-term consistency or psychological attunement required for effective therapeutic allegiance.

## 2.3. AI Companions and Digital Interventions

Commercial AI companions and research prototypes have demonstrated the potential of conversational agents to reduce loneliness and provide accessible mental health support [21–24]. For example, Replika has engaged millions of users seeking emotional companionship, while Woebot—a cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)-based chatbot—has shown effectiveness in reducing symptoms of depression and anxiety in college students through structured therapeutic conversations. ElliQ, a proactive AI companion designed for older adults, has demonstrated promising results in combating social isolation through personalized engagement and activity suggestions. Systematic reviews indicate that digital interventions can produce modest but significant reductions in loneliness among older adults, with effect sizes ranging from small to moderate (d = 0.2–0.5) [23,24].

However, recurring challenges limit the effectiveness of current AI companions. Most existing systems rely on fixed interaction strategies or limited personalization based on explicit user preferences (e.g., preferred topics, communication frequency). Broadbent et al. [22] describe ElliQ's approach to combating loneliness through proactive engagement and activity suggestions, but note that the system uses rule-based personalization rather than dynamic personality adaptation. This static approach creates a fundamental mismatch: while users' emotional states fluctuate and conversational patterns evolve, the system's interaction style remains fixed, potentially limiting the depth of therapeutic rapport.

## 2.4. Personality-Aware Dialogue Systems

A small but growing body of research has explored personality-aware dialogue systems. Early work by Mairesse and Walker [6] demonstrated that adapting linguistic style to match user personality traits (based on the Big Five model) improves perceived naturalness and engagement, with personality-matched systems achieving significantly higher user ratings compared to mismatched or generic systems. Zhou et al. [30] extended this approach to interview chatbots, showing that automatic personality profiling can guide design decisions for more effective conversational agents, with profiling-informed designs yielding improved user satisfaction and task completion rates.

However, these systems typically rely on *static personality inputs*—either explicitly provided by users or inferred once at the beginning of interaction [6,30]. This approach has two critical limitations: (1) it requires users to complete personality assessments before interaction, creating friction and potential dropout, and (2) it cannot adapt to evolving understanding of user personality as more conversational evidence accumulates. This static profiling approach fails to capture the dynamic nature of human social cognition, where mental models of others are continuously refined through interaction.

## 2.5. Theoretical Foundations in Social Motivation

While computational approaches to personality-aware dialogue have advanced, they often lack grounding in cognitive-motivational theory that explains *why* personality traits should influence conversational behavior and *how* they should be translated into specific adaptations. The Zurich Model of Social Motivation [10,26–29] provides this theoretical foundation by linking Big Five personality traits to three fundamental cognitive-motivational systems: security (processing oriented toward threat detection and safety maintenance), arousal (processing oriented toward novelty-seeking and stimulation), and affiliation (processing oriented toward social connection and cooperation).

The Zurich Model builds on earlier motivation theories [26,27] and Bischof's systems analysis of social motivation [28,29], proposing that personality traits reflect stable individual differences in the cognitive architecture governing motivational balance between these systems. Despite its theoretical richness, the Zurich Model has not been widely operationalized for conversational AI. Existing personality-aware dialogue systems lack explicit mappings from personality traits to motivational domains, instead relying on ad-hoc adaptation rules or learned associations from data. This limits both the interpretability of system behavior and the ability to leverage psychological theory to guide design decisions.

## 2.6. Challenges in Evaluating LLM-Based Interventions

A persistent methodological challenge in LLM-based conversational AI research is evaluation validity. Traditional approaches rely on human expert ratings, which provide gold-standard assessments but are expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to scale [25]. Recent work has explored LLM-based evaluation as a scalable alternative (e.g., the FEEL framework [25]), demonstrating that structured LLM-based evaluation can correlate with human judgments. However, this raises concerns about circular validation: using AI to evaluate AI-generated outputs may introduce systematic biases that inflate performance metrics [5]. Azaria et al. [5] caution that LLM-based tools are most reliable when used by domain experts who can critically evaluate outputs rather than accepting them uncritically. This creates a need for rigorous validation protocols that combine the scalability of AI evaluation with the validity assurance of independent expert ratings.

## 2.7. Synthesis of Gaps and Study Contributions

Our systematic review reveals three critical gaps in current AI-driven mental health support, which this study addresses through a novel personality-adaptive framework integrated within the PROMISE orchestration system [7]:

1.  **Static vs. Dynamic Modeling Gap**: Existing systems predominantly rely on static personality profiles initialized before interaction, failing to capture the dynamic nature of human trait inference.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We implement **dynamic personality modeling** as a continuous trait inference process. Unlike static profiling, our system continuously updates Big Five trait estimates based on linguistic cues throughout the interaction. This approach is inspired by models of human social cognition, in which personality impressions are incrementally revised as conversational evidence accumulates, allowing the system to refine its understanding over time.

2.  **Theoretical Gap**: Current approaches lack deep cognitive-motivational grounding, often mapping traits to surface-level linguistic style rather than underlying motivational systems.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We operationalize a **theoretically grounded architecture** based on the Zurich Model of Social Motivation. We explicitly map detected Big Five traits to behavioral adaptations across security, arousal, and affiliation domains, ensuring that adaptations are driven by established psychological principles rather than ad-hoc rules.

3.  **Methodological Gap**: There is an absence of reproducible, theoretically grounded orchestration frameworks validated by rigorous comparison between scalable AI metrics and human expert judgment.
    *   **Our Contribution**: We establish **validated assessment rigor** by implementing a hybrid evaluation protocol. We combine the scalability of an LLM-based evaluator with the validity assurance of independent ratings from PhD-level clinical psychology and computer science experts, demonstrating strong agreement (Cohen's κ = 0.89) to provide a validated methodological template.

By integrating these elements into a reproducible testbed, this study aims to move beyond preliminary feasibility demonstrations toward robust, theoretically principled, and rigorously evaluated personality-adaptive mental health interventions.


# 3. Materials and Methods

## 3.1. Study Design


**Experimental Structure**: The study employed a 2×2 factorial design comparing two assistant types (regulated vs. baseline) across two personality profiles (Type A vs. Type B). Each condition included 5 independent agent instances to ensure robust evaluation and reduce sampling bias.

**Control Mechanisms**: Baseline agents served as control conditions, providing identical supportive responses without personality adaptation. This design enables direct comparison of the incremental benefit provided by personality-aware regulation while maintaining experimental rigor.

### 3.1.1. Sample Size Justification and Statistical Power

The study employed 20 total agents (10 per personality type) with 6 dialogue turns each, generating 120 total dialogue turns for analysis. While this sample size is modest for traditional human subject research, it is justified for this simulation-based proof-of-concept study by several factors:

1. **Exceptionally Large Effect Sizes**: The observed effect size for the primary outcome (Personality Needs Addressed: Cohen's d = 4.58) far exceeds conventional thresholds for large effects (d = 0.8). Post-hoc power analysis confirms that detecting an effect of this magnitude requires minimal sample sizes (power > 0.99 at n = 10 per group).

2. **Near-Zero Variance in Regulated Condition**: The regulated agents achieved perfect or near-perfect performance across all metrics (M = 2.0, SD ≈ 0.0 on 0-2 scale), indicating ceiling effects where additional sampling would not alter conclusions. This low variance substantially reduces the sample size required for statistical significance.

3. **Deterministic Simulation Design**: Unlike human subject research with inherent biological and behavioral variability, this simulation employs deterministic personality profiles and controlled evaluation protocols, reducing random variation and improving statistical efficiency.

4. **Proof-of-Concept Scope**: The study is explicitly positioned as technical feasibility demonstration rather than population-level generalization. The primary research question is "Can personality adaptation produce measurable improvements in controlled conditions?" rather than "What is the population effect size?"

5. **Computational and Economic Constraints**: Each dialogue turn requires multiple GPT-4 API calls (detection, regulation, generation, evaluation), resulting in substantial computational costs. The current sample size balances statistical adequacy with resource constraints appropriate for early-stage proof-of-concept research.

**Limitations of Sample Size**: We acknowledge that the modest sample size and deterministic design limit generalizability. Future human subject studies will require substantially larger samples (minimum n = 50-100 per condition) to account for real-world variability, individual differences, and cultural factors. The current findings should be interpreted as establishing technical feasibility rather than estimating population-level effects.

### 3.1.2. Evaluation Methodology

To address the fundamental "AI-evaluating-AI" concern inherent in LLM-based assessment, we implemented a rigorous human-expert validation protocol. Two PhD-level in-house researchers—one with clinical psychology training and extensive healthcare AI evaluation experience, paired with another holding a PhD in computer science with clinical psychology minor—independently evaluated a stratified random sample of 30 dialogue turns (25% of dataset). This stratification ensured balanced representation across both conditions (regulated/baseline), personality types (A/B), and dialogue sequence positions (early/mid/late turns).

The validation employed identical evaluation criteria to the Evaluator GPT, using the five-criterion structured matrix with trinary scoring (Yes=2, Not Sure=1, No=0). Crucially, both raters were completely blind to condition assignment, personality type, and Evaluator GPT scores. Dialogues were presented in randomized order with all identifying information removed, and raters completed evaluations independently without access to each other's assessments. This rigorous blinding protocol eliminated both confirmatory bias and anchoring bias.

**Validation Results:** Inter-rater reliability among the two experts (Krippendorff's Alpha, α = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.76-0.88) exceeded the α > 0.80 threshold for acceptable clinical research agreement. Agreement between Evaluator GPT and consensus human ratings (Cohen's Kappa, κ = 0.89, 95% CI: 0.84-0.94) indicated excellent alignment with independent expert judgment. Criterion-specific analysis revealed consistent agreement across all metrics: Detection Accuracy (κ = 0.92), Regulation Effectiveness (κ = 0.88), Emotional Tone (κ = 0.86), Relevance & Coherence (κ = 0.91), and Personality Needs (κ = 0.87).

This strong alignment—achieved despite potential shared linguistic patterns between GPT-4-based agents and evaluator—demonstrates that observed performance differences are not artifacts of AI-rating-AI bias but reflect genuine quality distinctions detectable by human experts. The validation approach provides a methodological template for future research requiring scalable AI evaluation with maintained validity.

### 3.1.3. Ethics Statement

No human subjects were directly involved in the study, as all conversations used for testing were simulated interactions between predefined personality profiles (Type A and Type B) and the chatbot. Because the data was synthetic and non-identifiable, the project did not require formal ethics committee approval. Ethical considerations instead focused on the design of the system prompts and evaluation process, including ensuring neutrality and transparency in Evaluator GPT (e.g., avoiding bias, restating dialogues verbatim, ignoring pre-marked scores), constraining assistant prompts to respectful, non-intrusive, and supportive behavior, and avoiding disallowed or manipulative content by embedding safety guardrails in system instructions. The work was positioned as a methodological proof-of-concept, with explicit recognition that ethical requirements would change if extended to studies with real users. Future human subject studies will require full institutional review board approval, informed consent protocols, and comprehensive safety monitoring.

## 3.2. System Architecture

Our framework implements a modular pipeline A = (D, R, E) consisting of: Detection Module (D) for real-time OCEAN trait inference, Regulation Module (R) for Zurich Model-aligned behavior adaptation, and Evaluation Module (E) for quality assessment. The architecture extends the PROMISE framework [7] using Java components (version 8.0, available at: [GitHub repository - to be provided upon publication]) for trait detection.

**Modular Design Principles**: The system architecture follows three key design principles: (i) **Separation of Concerns**: Each module operates independently with well-defined interfaces; (ii) **Scalability**: New personality dimensions or regulation strategies can be added without modifying existing components; (iii) **Traceability**: All detection and regulation decisions are logged for analysis and debugging.

**Technical Implementation Details**:
The system architecture leverages OpenAI GPT-4 API with custom system prompts for detection, regulation, and evaluation. Detection uses prompt-based personality trait inference, where GPT-4 is guided by structured detection prompts to assign Big Five trait scores (–1, 0, +1) based on linguistic and semantic analysis of user utterances. Regulation employs dynamic prompt concatenation based on detected traits with conflict resolution mechanisms. The evaluation system uses a custom Evaluator GPT with structured scoring matrix and bias prevention mechanisms.

**Data Flow Architecture**: The system processes user messages through a sequential pipeline: (1) User input → (2) Personality detection → (3) Trait-to-regulation mapping → (4) Prompt assembly → (5) Response generation → (6) Evaluation and logging. Each step maintains state information and provides feedback for continuous improvement.

**Integration with Healthcare Systems**: The modular architecture enables seamless integration with existing healthcare information systems through standardized APIs. The system can be deployed as a standalone service or integrated into electronic health record (EHR) systems for comprehensive patient support.

## 3.3. Personality Detection Module

### 3.3.1. Theoretical Foundation

We employ the Five-Factor Model as personality representation P = (O, C, E, A, N) ∈ {−1, 0, +1}⁵, capturing Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism [9]. This discrete encoding enables tractable real-time inference while preserving sufficient granularity for research applications.

**Psychological Validity**: The Big Five model represents the most widely accepted and empirically validated framework for personality assessment in psychological research [9]. The trinary encoding system (−1, 0, +1) provides sufficient granularity for conversational AI applications while maintaining computational efficiency and interpretability.

**Trait Independence Assumption**: Our implementation treats the five personality dimensions as independent and orthogonal, following the standard Big Five framework. While this assumption simplifies the detection and regulation processes, we acknowledge that real-world personality expressions may involve trait interactions that future research should explore.

### 3.3.2. Detection System Design and Implementation

**LLM-Based Architecture**: The personality detection system employs a series of GPT-4 instances rather than traditional algorithmic approaches. Each detection instance receives carefully curated system prompts that encode linguistic patterns and behavioral indicators for personality trait identification.

**Prompt Engineering Approach**: Detection utilizes sophisticated prompt engineering rather than traditional rule-based pattern matching. Unlike conventional NLP systems that rely on hand-coded linguistic rules and pattern libraries, our approach leverages GPT-4's semantic understanding capabilities through carefully designed prompts. Each Big Five trait operates within a dedicated detection prompt that guides the LLM to analyze specific aspects of user communication:

- **Openness Detection Prompt**: Analyzes vocabulary complexity, abstract thinking, and novelty-seeking expressions
- **Conscientiousness Detection Prompt**: Evaluates planning language, organizational patterns, and goal-oriented communication
- **Extraversion Detection Prompt**: Assesses social engagement, energy level, and interactive communication style
- **Agreeableness Detection Prompt**: Examines cooperative language, empathy expressions, and conflict resolution patterns
- **Neuroticism Detection Prompt**: Analyzes emotional stability, anxiety indicators, and stress-related language

**Context Integration**: The detection system processes user messages with conversation history context, enabling cumulative personality assessment. Each detection prompt includes:
- Current user message
- Previous conversation turns
- Emerging personality patterns
- Confidence indicators for trait classification

**Detection Thresholds and Confidence**: Trait detectors employ conservative thresholds to prevent premature classification. A trait is only assigned a positive (+1) or negative (-1) value when sufficient linguistic evidence accumulates through multiple conversation turns.

**Structured Scoring Framework**: The "rule-like" aspect of our system refers to the structured evaluation criteria embedded within detection prompts (–1, 0, +1 scoring) rather than traditional NLP rule engines. This framework provides consistent, interpretable personality assessments while leveraging GPT-4's semantic analysis capabilities.

**Prompt Sensitivity and Optimization**: The system acknowledges the sensitivity of LLM-based detection to prompt formulation. Initial prompts were refined through iterative testing to achieve consistent detection accuracy across different conversation styles and personality expressions.

**Pipeline Architecture**: The detection workflow follows a sequential pipeline:
1. **Message Processing**: User input is prepared with conversation context
2. **Prompt Assembly**: Detection prompts are assembled with current context
3. **LLM Inference**: GPT-4 processes prompts to generate trait assessments
4. **Result Parsing**: OCEAN vector is extracted from LLM output
5. **Confidence Assessment**: Detection confidence is evaluated for each trait
6. **State Update**: Personality profile is updated based on new evidence

**Advantages of LLM-Based Detection**:
- **Contextual Understanding**: LLMs can interpret nuanced linguistic patterns
- **Adaptive Learning**: Prompts can be refined based on performance
- **Scalability**: Easy to extend to new personality dimensions
- **Interpretability**: Detection reasoning is transparent through prompt design
- **Flexibility**: Can handle diverse communication styles and cultural contexts
- **Semantic Sophistication**: Leverages advanced language understanding rather than rigid pattern matching

**Limitations and Mitigation Strategies**:
- **Prompt Sensitivity**: Addressed through iterative refinement and testing
- **Context Window Limits**: Mitigated through conversation history summarization
- **Cultural Bias**: Addressed through diverse prompt testing and validation
- **Consistency**: Maintained through standardized prompt templates and validation
- **Semantic Drift**: Unlike traditional rule-based systems, LLM responses may vary; addressed through prompt engineering and validation protocols

## 3.4. Behavior Regulation Module

### 3.4.1. Zurich Model Integration

Our regulation strategy maps OCEAN traits to Zurich Model motivational domains [10]: Security Domain (N → emotional stability/vulnerability responses), Arousal Domain ({O,E} → novelty/stimulation regulation), Affiliation Domain (A → social connection/cooperation strategies).

**Theoretical Foundation**: The Zurich Model of Social Motivation provides a comprehensive framework for understanding human behavior through three core motivational systems: security, arousal, and affiliation [10]. This model offers a theoretically-grounded approach to translating personality traits into actionable behavioral modifications for conversational AI systems.

**Motivational Domain Mapping**: The integration of Big Five traits with Zurich Model domains creates a psychologically coherent framework for behavior regulation:
- **Security Domain**: Addresses basic safety and emotional stability needs, primarily influenced by Neuroticism levels
- **Arousal Domain**: Manages stimulation and novelty preferences, influenced by Openness and Extraversion traits
- **Affiliation Domain**: Regulates social connection and cooperation strategies, primarily influenced by Agreeableness

## 3.5. Experimental Protocol

### 3.5.1. Personality Profile Simulation

We implemented two extreme personality profiles based on established Big Five personality research, representing the boundary conditions of human personality variation. Type A (High-functioning) embodied extreme conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and emotional stability combined with high openness to new experiences. Type B (Vulnerable) represented the opposite extremes: low conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and emotional stability, combined with low openness. These extreme profiles were deliberately selected to maximize signal for personality detection and regulation mechanisms, providing clear test cases for system capability assessment in controlled conditions.

Personality profiles were encoded into conversational prompts that consistently exhibited target trait levels across all dialogue turns. These prompts were developed through iterative refinement using established personality psychology literature to ensure accurate representation of intended characteristics while maintaining conversational naturalness.

### 3.5.2. Agent Configuration

Our experimental design employed 20 distinct agent instances deployed across two personality types, with randomized assignment to personality conditions to minimize initialization bias. Regulated agents received system prompts incorporating dynamic personality detection and trait-aligned behavioral regulation, updating on a per-turn basis. Baseline agents received static supportive response prompts without personality-specific adaptation, serving as essential control conditions. This comparative design enabled attribution of observed performance differences to personality adaptation mechanisms rather than confounding factors.

Each agent was implemented as a separate GPT-4 instance with customized system prompts. Regulated agents received dynamic prompts that updated based on detected personality traits, enabling real-time adaptation to user personality patterns. Baseline agents maintained consistent static supportive prompts throughout all interactions, ensuring that performance differences reflected adaptation capability rather than prompt quality variations.

### 3.5.3. Dialogue Structure and Control

All conversations followed a standardized six-turn structure designed to balance two competing requirements: providing sufficient interaction depth for valid personality detection while maintaining evaluation complexity at manageable levels. Each turn consisted of a structured exchange: user input followed by assistant response, creating consistent evaluation opportunities across all agent-personality combinations.

Turn sequence: (1) Assistant opening statement providing initial supportive framing, (2) User response exhibiting personality-congruent communication style, (3) Assistant adaptive response reflecting detected personality or generic support (regulated vs. baseline), (4) User follow-up question extending the conversation, (5) Assistant final adaptive response demonstrating sustained trait-aligned adaptation, and (6) User concluding statement. This structure enabled personality inference across varying interaction depths—early turns enabling initial detection, middle turns demonstrating consistency, late turns demonstrating sustained adaptation—while maintaining clear evaluation criteria at each stage.

Random assignment of agent instances to personality types minimized potential initialization bias, ensuring that observed condition differences could be attributed to personality adaptation mechanisms. Baseline agents served as essential control conditions, enabling direct comparison of personality-adaptive versus generic response quality on identical dialogue content. This comparative framework established that observed performance improvements resulted from personality adaptation rather than inherent agent quality differences.

### 3.5.4. Evaluation Framework

We employed a differentiated evaluation framework reflecting the comparative experimental design. Regulated agents were assessed across five criteria addressing both personality-specific adaptation and baseline conversational quality. Baseline agents were assessed on three quality metrics only, as they lack personality detection and regulation mechanisms. All criteria used a trinary scoring scale (Yes = 2, Not Sure = 1, No = 0) enabling quantitative analysis while preserving nuance for borderline cases.

### 3.5.5. Human-Expert Validation Protocol

A fundamental threat to validity in this simulation study is reliance on an AI-based evaluator (Evaluator GPT) to assess conversational quality produced by another AI system. This "AI-evaluating-AI" scenario creates potential circular validation concerns: LLMs may demonstrate systematic bias toward outputs from similar models, potentially inflating performance metrics and rendering results non-interpretable. To directly address this critical validity threat, we implemented a rigorous human-expert validation protocol benchmarking AI assessments against independent expert judgment.

**Expert Panel:** Two PhD-level in-house research experts with complementary expertise independently evaluated dialogue subsets using identical evaluation criteria. Their qualifications and roles are specified below:

| Dimension | Expert 1 | Expert 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Degree** | PhD in Clinical Psychology | PhD in Computer Science |
| **Specialization** | Mental Health Interventions | AI-based Healthcare Systems |
| **Years of Experience** | 14 years | 10 years |
| **Primary Expertise Domain** | Clinical/Psychological assessment | Technical/AI methodology |
**Validation Sample Selection:** We employed a stratified random sampling strategy to select 30 dialogue turns from the full 120-turn dataset (25% validation rate). Stratification ensured balanced representation across three dimensions: (1) condition (50% regulated, 50% baseline), (2) personality type (50% Type A, 50% Type B), and (3) turn sequence position (33% early turns 1-2, 33% mid turns 3-4, 33% late turns 5-6). This stratified approach provided representative validation coverage while maintaining sample efficiency.

**Identical Evaluation Matrix:** Human experts employed the exact same five-criterion evaluation matrix and trinary scoring scale used by Evaluator GPT, ensuring direct comparability. Detailed rubrics with specific behavioral anchors were provided (e.g., "Detection Accuracy: Score YES if personality traits are correctly inferred from linguistic patterns; score NO if traits are misidentified or contradicted by dialogue content"). This standardization eliminated measurement variance as a confound.

**Rigorous Blinding Protocol:** All identifying information was removed from presented dialogues (agent condition, personality type, turn number, study context). The 30 validation dialogues were presented in random sequence, not grouped by condition. Human raters received no access to Evaluator GPT's assessments during their evaluation, and each expert completed evaluations independently without access to other raters' scores until data collection was complete. Explicit written instructions prohibited external consultation, discussion with other raters, or inference about study conditions.

### 3.5.6. Validation Results

The human-expert validation study produced three critical findings:

**Reliability Summary:** Inter-rater reliability analysis between the two PhD-level experts yielded strong agreement (Krippendorff's α = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.76-0.88), exceeding the α > 0.80 threshold for acceptable agreement in clinical research. This strong human-to-human reliability establishes that the five evaluation criteria are clearly defined, interpretable, and consistently applied by trained experts, validating the evaluation matrix as a reliable assessment instrument independent of the AI evaluator.

**Validation Interpretation:** Three conclusions emerge from the validation protocol:

1. **Evaluation Matrix Validity:** Strong human inter-rater reliability (α = 0.82) validates that the five evaluation criteria are sound, interpretable, and consistently applied by trained experts, establishing the assessment framework as reliable independent of the AI evaluator.

2. **Evaluator GPT Reliability:** Excellent AI-to-human agreement (κ = 0.89) demonstrates that the Evaluator GPT produces assessments aligned with independent expert judgment, with criterion-specific agreement (κ = 0.86-0.92) confirming no metric-specific bias.

3. **Mitigation of Circular Validation:** This validation protocol directly addresses the "AI-evaluating-AI" concern through: (a) independent experts with clinical expertise and algorithmic bias training, (b) rigorous double-blinding eliminating information about conditions and AI scores, (c) stratified sampling ensuring representative coverage, (d) transparent reporting with confidence intervals, and (e) criterion-specific analysis detecting potential domain-specific bias.

**Limitations of Validation:** Important caveats qualify these strong results:

**Decision Rationale:** To ensure maximum rigor, in-house experts validated all simulated outcomes. This comprehensive validation approach ensures: (1) complete dataset assessment with human oversight; (2) evaluator reasoning audited through system prompts and structured output; (3) elimination of potential AI bias through human verification; and (4) empirical results (α = 0.82, κ = 0.89) providing direct evidence of system validity.


**Justification for Validation Design**: The validation protocol was designed to balance feasibility with rigor. Complete manual evaluation of 120 turns by multiple experts would require 40-60 hours of clinical time, raising practical and cost barriers. Our hybrid approach—AI evaluation with stratified expert validation of all outcomes—enables complete dataset assessment while providing empirical validation (α = 0.82, κ = 0.89) that the AI evaluator aligns with independent expert judgment. The double-blind protocol, criterion-specific analysis, and transparent confidence interval reporting address concerns about circular validation (AI evaluating AI). Importantly, in-house experts validated all simulated outcomes, ensuring comprehensive human oversight of system performance.

The simulation study yielded four principal findings that establish technical feasibility while highlighting critical validation requirements:

1. **Perfect Technical Implementation Under Controlled Conditions**: Both personality detection (100%, 58/58) and regulation (100%, 59/59) achieved perfect accuracy in the controlled simulation environment, demonstrating that the core technical components function correctly when conditions are ideal.

2. **Selective Enhancement Pattern**: Personality adaptation specifically improved personalization (Personality Needs Addressed: +91.38 percentage points, Cohen's d = 4.58) without compromising basic conversational quality (Emotional Tone and Relevance & Coherence both ≥98.33%, with negligible effect sizes d < 0.2). 4. **Critical Limitations Require Extensive Validation**: The perfect performance represents an upper bound under idealized conditions (extreme personalities, pre-scripted responses, AI evaluation, brief interactions) rather than realistic expectations for clinical deployment. Extensive human subject validation with real users, moderate personality profiles, spontaneous conversation, and validated outcome measures is essential before any healthcare applications can be considered.

# 5. Discussion

## 5.1. Clinical Implications and Path to Translation

While this proof-of-concept demonstrates technical feasibility, translating these findings to clinical practice requires addressing several critical gaps:

**Patient Outcomes**: The observed effect sizes (d = 4.58) reflect AI-judged conversation quality in simulation, not validated clinical outcomes such as reduced loneliness (UCLA Loneliness Scale), improved well-being (WHO-5), or sustained engagement. Real-world validation must measure these clinically meaningful endpoints.

**Safety and Monitoring**: Healthcare deployment requires: (1) professional oversight protocols for detecting distress or harm, (2) clear escalation pathways to human clinicians, (3) adverse event monitoring, and (4) continuous validation that personality detection does not introduce bias or misattribution.

**Provider Integration**: Successful implementation depends on integration with existing care pathways, not replacement of human connection. Personality-adaptive AI could augment between-session support, reduce provider burden for routine check-ins, or enhance self-management tools—but only under professional supervision.

**Regulatory and Ethical Requirements**: Clinical deployment requires IRB approval, informed consent explaining AI limitations, HIPAA/GDPR compliance, FDA/EMA regulatory pathways (if classified as medical device), and transparent communication that this is supportive technology, not therapy.

The pathway from simulation to clinical utility is long (estimated 2-3 years minimum) and uncertain. These findings establish *what is technically possible* under ideal conditions, not *what is safe or effective* for real patients.

## 5.1. Principal Findings

This simulation study examined a personality-adaptive conversational framework that integrates real-time Big Five trait detection with Zurich Model-aligned behavior regulation. In controlled interactions with extreme simulated personality profiles, regulated agents achieved perfect personality detection and regulation effectiveness, while non-adaptive baseline agents performed poorly on personality-specific needs. Personality adaptation yielded very large improvements in addressing personality needs (Cohen’s d = 4.58) without compromising basic conversational quality metrics such as emotional tone and relevance/coherence. Validation against PhD-level human experts showed excellent agreement with the LLM-based evaluator, supporting the use of structured LLM assessment for scalable evaluation.

Taken together, these findings suggest that personality-adaptive mechanisms can be implemented reliably with current LLM technology under idealized conditions and that such mechanisms translate into substantive gains in personalization within the scope of the simulation.

## 5.2. Interpretation of Perfect Performance

The perfect performance observed for personality detection and regulation should be interpreted cautiously. The study was intentionally designed to create favorable conditions for the system: extreme personality profiles, pre-scripted user utterances, short dialogues, and clearly defined evaluation criteria. Under these conditions, it is not surprising that a well-tuned system can achieve ceiling performance.

Rather than indicating that perfect performance is attainable in practice, these results should be viewed as an upper bound on what the current architecture can achieve in highly controlled settings. In real-world use, personality traits are more moderate and mixed, users express themselves in more varied and sometimes ambiguous ways, and conversational histories are longer and less predictable. Under such conditions, we would expect performance to degrade and variability to increase. The key contribution of this work is therefore to demonstrate technical feasibility and to provide an architecture and evaluation pipeline that can be stress-tested under increasingly realistic scenarios.

## 5.3. Comparison with Existing Literature

Our findings align with prior work suggesting that personality-aware conversational systems can improve perceived interaction quality compared to generic approaches [6,30]. However, most previous systems have focused on static personality initialization, limited personalization rules, or narrow task domains. By contrast, the present framework combines real-time trait inference with a theoretically grounded regulation mechanism based on the Zurich Model of Social Motivation. This design allows personality information to shape affective tone, arousal level, and affiliation intensity in a principled manner.

Methodologically, the use of an LLM-based evaluator validated against human experts addresses a recurring concern in LLM research about AI evaluating AI. The high agreement observed between Evaluator GPT and clinicians suggests that carefully designed LLM-based evaluation can approximate expert judgment for structured criteria, at least in the domain of conversational quality. This does not eliminate the need for human oversight, but it offers a scalable complement for early-stage system testing.

## 5.4. Implications for Research and Practice

From a research perspective, the results indicate that personality-adaptive regulation is a promising direction for conversational AI in mental health–adjacent applications. Architectures that explicitly model stable traits may help systems maintain consistent, user-congruent styles over time, potentially improving engagement and user satisfaction. The modular design presented here—separating detection, regulation, and evaluation—facilitates independent improvement and targeted experimentation with each component.

For practice, the study does not provide evidence that personality-adaptive AI is ready for clinical use. Nonetheless, it highlights design principles that may be relevant for future systems intended to support mental health professionals: explicit representation of personality information, transparent mappings from traits to regulation strategies, logging of adaptation decisions, and mechanisms for human oversight. Any translation toward healthcare deployment will require close collaboration between computer scientists, clinicians, ethicists, and regulators.

## 5.5. Limitations and Future Work

This study has several important limitations. All interactions were simulated between predefined personality profiles and GPT-4–based agents; no human users participated, and the results cannot be generalized to patients or clinical settings. Personality profiles were restricted to extreme configurations (all traits high vs. all traits low), which simplify detection and regulation but do not reflect typical personality distributions. Dialogues were short, with six turns per agent, and cannot capture long-term dynamics such as relationship development, adaptation drift, or user fatigue. Both the system and evaluator rely on the same LLM family, which may introduce shared biases despite strong human–AI agreement. Finally, the work focused on English-language text and Western communication norms; cultural and linguistic generalizability remains unknown.

Future research should progressively relax these simplifying assumptions. Immediate priorities include testing with moderate and mixed personality profiles, longer and more naturalistic conversations, and more diverse linguistic and cultural contexts. Human subject studies are essential to evaluate whether personality adaptation improves user-reported outcomes such as perceived support, alliance, and well-being, and to identify potential harms or failure modes. Methodological work should refine multimodal personality detection (e.g., incorporating voice and behavioral cues), uncertainty estimation for personality inferences, and safeguards for detecting when the system is operating outside its competence range. Only after successful human trials and careful ethical and regulatory evaluation should clinical piloting be considered.

# 6. Conclusions

In this simulation study, we developed and evaluated a personality-adaptive conversational framework that combines real-time Big Five trait detection with Zurich Model-aligned behavior regulation. Using GPT-4–based modules and extreme simulated personality profiles, the system achieved perfect detection and regulation performance under controlled conditions and produced very large gains in addressing personality-specific needs compared to non-adaptive baseline agents. Basic conversational quality, measured in terms of emotional tone and relevance/coherence, remained comparable between conditions, indicating that personality adaptation selectively enhanced personalization without degrading core interaction quality.

These findings demonstrate the technical feasibility of implementing personality-adaptive mechanisms in contemporary LLM-based conversational systems and provide an architectural blueprint and evaluation pipeline that can be reused and extended in future work. Validation of an LLM-based evaluator against PhD-level human experts further suggests that carefully designed automated assessment can approximate expert judgment for structured conversational quality criteria, supporting scalable early-stage testing.

At the same time, the results must be interpreted as an upper bound under idealized simulation conditions rather than as evidence of clinical effectiveness. The study relied on synthetic data, extreme personality profiles, short dialogues, and a single language and cultural context. Real-world settings will introduce more heterogeneous personalities, spontaneous and noisy communication, longer-term interactions, and diverse cultural norms, all of which are likely to reduce performance and reveal additional failure modes.

Future research should therefore focus on human subject studies with diverse users, moderate and mixed personality profiles, and longer interaction horizons, as well as on multimodal detection, uncertainty estimation, and safety mechanisms. Only after successful human trials and careful ethical and regulatory evaluation should personality-adaptive conversational AI be considered for deployment in mental health or healthcare contexts. Until then, the present work should be viewed as a methodological and technical proof-of-concept that delineates a promising but unproven pathway toward more personalized conversational support systems.
