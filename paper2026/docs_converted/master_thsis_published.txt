The energy sector is the source of nearly three-quarters of the global greenhouse gas emissions and buildings
form around one-third of it. The largest share of the energy consumption in the buildings belongs to the heating
systems which are mostly dependent on fossil fuels. To keep up with the demands of increasing population, it is
vital to undergo clean energy transformations effective immediately. In this study, a decision support map was
created for a selected community to ease the energy retrofit candidate selection process. Buildings are assessed
considering sustainability level of the heating infrastructure, energy use intensity and age, and ranked according
to their goodness level on the energy efficiency scale through analytic hierarchy process. Finally, a multi-layered
map is created to visualize the outcomes. The decision support map fills a gap in the existing tool landscape as it
offers energy consulting on a communal (i.e. not only single building or quarter) level and does not require work
from the user as it relies on public data. The results showed that new buildings constructed by environmentally
responsible codes tend to have heating systems mostly powered by renewable sources and less level of energy
threshold violations while old buildings generally act in the other direction. As the main outcome, the created
visuals support the user to have insights about the accumulation areas of the households in low rankings as well
as the energy efficiency standing of the individual buildings in comparison with the neighbouring households.

1. Introduction
Buildings account for approximately one-third of the global energy
consumption and a quarter of CO2 emissions [1,2]. In EU, households
formed 27 % of the final energy consumption in 2020 of which a share of
62.8 % was consumed for space heating followed by hot water prepa­
ration with a share of 15.1 % [3]. The proportions are comparable for
Switzerland: a share of 68.5 % of the final energy consumption by pri­
vate households was consumed for space heating and 13.4 % was
consumed for water heating [4].
Considering the energy resources, fossil fuels are the most frequently
used ones in the heating sector, and renewable energy sources were used
to meet barely a quarter of the global heat demand in 2021 [5]. For
instance, the largest share of the global energy demand was covered by
natural gas accounting for 42 % of heating energy needs [6]. While this
is also valid for EU on average [3], Switzerland slightly differs with a
value of 60 % of heating demand shared approximately equally between
natural gas and fuel oil [4].
With the population growth, meeting this increasingly high demand

that is heavily dependent on non-renewable resources inevitably results
in excessive amount of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions which even­
tually accelerates the global warming [7,8]. In addition to UNDP, the
European directive 2024/1275 ratified in 2024 [9] that requires EU
countries to ensure that all new buildings were designed and constructed
as nearly zero-energy buildings (NZEB) effective for private buildings
from the end of 2030 and for public buildings from the end of 2028. A
NZEB means that the building has a very high energy performance and
very low (nearly zero) energy demand which is supplied highly by
renewable resources that are produced on-site or nearby [10]. Addi­
tionally, all member countries shall develop a long-term renovation
strategy for all existing buildings that facilitates the cost-effective
transformation to highly energy efficient and decarbonized building
stocks by 2050 [9]. Furthermore, the directive 2024/1275, necessitates
that the 16/26 % of the least efficient buildings are renovated to meet
the threshold value for energy efficiency [11].

mand in the households increased around 4 % compared to 2020 (3 %
compared with 2019) which is the largest annual increase in the last

An overview over the current research and available tools is given in
[22]. The currently published approaches differ along three different
orthogonal directions: First, the spatial scale of the system considered, i.
e. from single buildings, quarters to whole communities [23]. Second,
the complexity of the energy system model ranging from simple energy
sums over energy balance models to complex non-linear system models.
Third, the demand of data that has to be supplied to start the analysis
[24,25]: most toolkits require the provision of extensive data sets both
about the building and energy system component properties as well as
the individual consumption profiles.
The majority of studies focusses on individual buildings: many
studies were conducted on finding the optimal energy retrofit solution
using different objectives and various solution methods. Most used ob­
jectives are the energy consumption/energy saving, cost optimality
considering lifecycle analysis, the thermal comfort or combination of
these [21,26]. Various energy simulation tools are used in these studies
to assist the selection of the best option such as eQUEST [27], Ener­
gyPlus [28], TRNSYS [29] and Retrosim [30].
On the other hand, considering the entire building block perspective,
the studies in the literature are not as well-developed as it is for single
buildings. Energy performance of a building is one of the key criteria in
candidate selection for retrofitting buildings and it is dependent on
various factors such as location, climate conditions, heating infrastruc­
ture, building envelope, occupant comfort etc. [21]. Regarding the en­
ergy assessment in block level, energy consumptions are often calculated
using detailed building information which makes the method heavily
data-driven [31]. Though there are models that use various approaches
to simulate energy consumption for the entire block, there exist
considerable limitations such as the challenge of accessing adequate and
accurate information for all the buildings in the large blocks, long
simulation times and the accuracy of the simulations [32]. As it is hard
to access adequate and accurate information for all the buildings in the
large blocks, the methods are often based on reference buildings,
building categories, building types, typologies or example case studies
[33,34]. Furthermore, for the optimisation of districts, multiple criteria
have to be considered and the different performance indicators such as
energy consumption, greenhouse gas emissions and investment costs
have to be balanced. As the individual performance indicators are often
even contradictory, a decision process has to be installed to consider the
individual aspects/indicators in the user desired weighting. A popular
approach to implement this approach is an analytical hierarchical pro­
cess that defines a new cost function (optimisation target) where the
individual contributions are combined according to the desired
weighting [35].
As last spatial scale we consider the investigation of the whole
community or urban area: Evaluating the energy performance of
buildings in urban scale by using Geographic Information Systems (GIS)
gained importance in the literature after nearly 2012 [36] and is utilized
in both the modelling [37] and assessment [38] fields. In general, the
GIS tool is used to describe the distribution of energy consumption in
block level which ease the analysis on retrofitting of buildings and po­
tential energy savings [39] as well as the potential energy supplies from
the renewable energy sources [40]. Considering the application areas of
GIS in this topic, it acts as an effective tool for decision supporting [41].
Due to the better availability of data often also a strong focus on met­
ropoles as test cases for the developed methodologies are chosen, e.g.
Rome [23].

1.1. Literature review
The efficiency of building retrofitting mainly depends on two main
steps, decision making of which building will be chosen and what
retrofit strategy suits the selected candidate the most. Considering the
urgency of the transformations and the high level of investments, allo­
cating necessary sources to candidates which will show the maximum
improvement has the utmost importance for the policy makers. Knowing
the energy efficiency distribution of the households within their com­
munity would enable them to adjust the public policies, interest rates,
direct supports or tax discounts in most effective ways which will
maximize commonwealth in the long run. Moreover, it will ease the
retrofit decision-making process of the house owners to know the
standing of their buildings in the goodness scale on energy efficiency.
Through proper retrofitting, house owners would economically benefit
from the significant reduction in their energy consumptions [17,18]. A
particular challenge here is to balance the interests at different spatial
scales. Single house owners may have substantially different aims
compared to district owners and both groups might have different
detailed aims compared to a communal energy planning.
An idealized retrofit process is composed of energy assessment of the
building, identification of the problematic systems, evaluation of energy
conservation benefits, determination of the retrofit level, economic
analysis, risk assessment and estimation of energy savings [19]. Among

3.1. The federal buildings and housing register (RBD) dataset
For the presented case study, the community ‘Wittenbach’ was
selected, which is a municipality in the canton of St. Gallen close to the
capital St. Gallen in Switzerland. The building properties which are
registered in the canton of St. Gallen were downloaded from the Federal
Office for Statistics − the Federal Register of Buildings and Dwellings
(RBD) [42]. The dataset contains 47 columns of features including
building identifiers (EGID), address details, coordinates, construction
and if available renovation years, number of floors, heating types, etc. In
total, 217,923 rows of buildings are contained in this data set of which
2538 belong to Wittenbach. Descriptions and details of the features were
taken from the Characteristics Catalogue published by the Federal Office
for Statistics [43]. In the reference period of 2001 – 2020, Wittenbach
had a mean temperature of 9.4 ◦ C, 113 cooling degree days, 3414
heating degree days and 87 days below 0 ◦ C and a precipitation of 1225
mm [44].

the construction year as clustered in bins of 5 years [43].

3.1.2.6. Heating grade. Existing heating infrastructure types for build­
ings play a major role in the ranking of the system. It is essential to
supply energy through renewable resources (sun, air, water etc.) instead
of non-renewable ones (fossil fuels) considering sustainability and
environmental point of view. So, the more the heating system depends
on renewable sources, the higher the sustainability rating of the building
should be. Likewise, the opposite is also true; more non-renewable
powered systems lead to a lower score.
To quantify this concept, a grading algorithm was constructed that
varies between [-2, +2]. The more the system is supplied with nonrenewable resources, the higher the grade becomes. Types of heating
systems, their energy sources [43] and corresponding grades assigned to
each can be found in Table 3, below. Sustainable categories are marked
with greener shades, non-renewable sources with more red shades.
In the cases where the building involves two different heating sys­
tems (i.e., existence of secondary heating system), the total grade was
calculated by summing up the individual grades. Therefore, the general
grading interval became [-4, +4].

3.1.2.2. Building class. Building classes are labelled with 26 distinct
codes to clarify the type and utilization of the structures, such as
building with one apartment, hotel building, farm buildings etc. [43].
3.1.2.3. Residential share. The share of residential usage is classified
according to the amount of residential usage in the buildings with 4
unique codes. The code 1020 means that the building is exclusively
residential used (100 % residential use), the code 1030 indicates that the
residential share is between 50–99 %, the code 1040 signify the share is
between 1–50 % and the code 1060 implies the building is completely
commercial (0 % residential use) [43]. To analytically calculate the total
residential area within a building (Section 2.2.3), the share intervals
were replaced with their average value and the corresponding factor was
multiplied with the building area (cf. Sec. 2.1.3 for details). Hence, for
the code 1030 a factor of 75 % residential was taken and for the code
1040 a factor of 25 % residential was taken.
Different categories of buildings, such as residential and commercial,
display distinct patterns in energy usages. To be able to analyze each
group individually, a labelling system was created. The buildings with a
residential share of 100 % were categorized as ‘Residential’, while the
ones with 0 % were named as ‘Commercial’. The rest in between were
labelled as ‘Partially Residential’.

3.2.3. Assigning demands to households
The heat demand values (commercial, residential and total) of a
polygon are calculated as the cumulative demands of each household
that is located within the corresponding polygon [53]. For example, as
can be seen from Table 6, the polygon with the id 211,029 has a total
heat demand of 72.9 MWh/a which is the sum of the demands of each
building inside the polygon boundaries.
In order to distribute demands over the individual households, it
must first be determined which house belongs to which polygon. With
this aim, a spatial merge was carried out between the locations of
polygons and buildings. As a result of the merge, 1474 among 1636
households were assigned to their corresponding polygons. The
remaining 162 buildings were unassigned as there were no demand data
retrieved for their locations from the SFOE dataset.
Secondly, for each assigned building, the ERA was divided into res­
idential and commercial parts according to the residential share of the
entry (cf. Section 2.1.2.3 for definition). Then, for each polygon, the
total residential and total commercial areas were calculated by summing
the corresponding areas of each building within the polygon boundaries.
Next, for each polygon, residential and commercial specific heating
energy demands (SHED) (MWh/m2/a) were calculated by splitting the
residential and commercial heat demands (MWh/a) according to the
total respective area.
The final step is to assign SHED values to the buildings. Three basic
rules were considered during this assignment. If the building is 100 %
residential, then the total SHED of the building was taken as the resi­
dential SHED value of the polygon it is in. Likewise, if the building is
100 % commercial, then the total SHED of the building was taken as the
commercial SHED value of the polygon it is in. On the other hand, if the
building is partially residential, then the total SHED value of the

3.2. Swiss Federal Office of Energy (SFOE) dataset
The second property/dataset required for constructing the ranking
system was the energy demands of the households in Wittenbach, St.
Gallen. The Swiss Federal Office of Energy (SFOE) [48] published the
heatmap of energy demands from residential and commercial buildings
for Switzerland. The source data for the map is provided by the Verband
Fernwärme Schweiz (VFS) and is based on the information from the RBD
[49]. This source data can be accessed by the GeoAdmin API which al­
lows the usage of geospatial information provided by the Swiss Federal
Administration [50]. For this map layer, a call to the API returns the
geospatial information of designated polygons along with 11 attributes
such as polygon identifiers, total yearly demand, yearly residential and
commercial demands.
3.2.1. Preprocessing
In order to call the API, it is required to define the interested area by
specifying the corresponding coordinates. The geometry definition can

present-day conditions. In the referenced study, the energy demand

For this study, threshold values for the SHED were taken as the energy

3.4. Analytical hierarchical process
To select the optimum candidates for retrofitting multiple perfor­
mance indicators (e.g. age, heating grade etc.) have to be considered. To
ensure a fair consideration of all these aspects, here, an analytical hi­
erarchical process (AHP) is considered [35]. This analytic hierarchy
process (AHP) was applied as it is a widely used method for multicriteria
analysis. This field has to be considered to ensure that the best candidate
or ranking of the alternatives is achieved by considering a set of criteria
over possible alternatives. Application of the technique consists of
determining the required indicators and their importance weights over
the selected aim.
In this study, it was aimed to rank the households towards finding the
most suitable candidates for retrofitting by considering their heating
grade, threshold violation and building age (Fig. 1). The importance
factors of the criteria were represented as w1, w2 and w3. For simplicity,
here all weights were chosen equally with the value of 1/3. Depending
on the preferences of the customer the choice can be adjusted to ensure
that the chosen indicators and their priority are respected.
All three criteria that were used in AHP process had difference in
their ranges. While heating grade is integer and varies from − 4 to 4,
threshold violation and building age varies in hundreds which might
lead to unbalanced decisive influence on the goal. To bring all features in
the same standing, scaling was applied to the criteria. As a scaling
method, min–max scaler was selected (Eq.3) and the ranges of all
criteria were transformed into [0, 1].

5. Conclusions
In this study, a decision support map was created for a selected
community (Wittenbach) to ease the building retrofit candidate selec­
tion process. The presented solution has three major advantages: the tool
can easily be adapted for any Swiss community independent of its size, it
enables also non-expert persons to readily identify suitable candidate for
retrofitting and it does not require any work for the collection of the data
to obtain a first estimation.
As the first step, the households within the community were evalu­
ated on sustainability level of their heating infrastructure, severity of
their energy use intensity and building age. Then, the buildings were
graded and ranked according to their goodness (or badness) level on the
energy efficiency scale through analytic hierarchy process (AHP). The
evaluation of the energy demand required an estimation of the energy
demand for residential and commercial buildings. The comparison with
two reference data sets (national study of photovoltaic potential and
energy report of the community) indicated that the aggregated demands
can be estimated up to 25 % and 1 %. The substantial deviation for the
national study can be explained by the difference in the base data set (in
the national study industrial demands are considered as well). If the
contribution of the industrial demand in included the deviation is
reduced to 1.8 %. Finally, the outcomes were visualized on the com­
munity map by individual layers representing different criteria and
building categories.
In the results, it was seen that in general manner all three criteria
effect the households similarly on the goodness scale. Both with the help
of new construction codes that are restrictive in energy concept and
increased environmental public awareness, new buildings have rather
good heating grades and less energy threshold violations.
The energy threshold values selected for this study were corre­
sponding to the conditions in 2016 and with this configuration more
than half of the buildings do not have violation (percentual deviation of
the estimated specific heat energy demand from a reference threshold in
the corresponding building category). Moreover, the number of house­
holds decreases strongly with increasing value of violation. Considering
the worldwide emphasis on increasing the pace in clean energy trans­
formation rates to reach the targeted values, employing lower threshold
values that are correlated with the minimum energy demands can also
be studied as a future extension of this study.
The multi-layered decision support map with interactive details
established in this work is beneficial for both house owners and policy

its applicability is requiring a comparable public data source which is
currently only available for Switzerland and multiple regions in Ger­

assessment are based on the quality of the public datasets, the quality of

source. As a second limitation it has to be considered that the calcula­

source is replaced by another one or its format is changed, the corre­

require substantial work.

decision-making process for retrofit, this study might further be
improved by implementing larger scale regions to increase the benefit.
Furthermore, combining with customized retrofit suggestions and
