Abstract
Objective. This thesis investigates whether dynamically regulating chatbot behavior
based on user personality traits improves the effectiveness of emotional support interactions.
Grounded in the Big Five (OCEAN) personality framework and the Zurich Model of Social
Motivation, the study implements a personality-adaptive chatbot system that detects user traits in
real time and adjusts its conversational tone, structure, and engagement strategy accordingly.
Experimental Design. Two extreme personality profiles‚Äîhigh OCEAN (Type A)
and low OCEAN (Type B)‚Äîwere simulated to evaluate system performance under contrasting
psychological conditions. A controlled experiment compared personality-adaptive chatbots
(regulated) with non-adaptive chatbots (baseline) across 10 simulated conversations. Regulated
chatbots continuously updated their personality detection after each user message and
dynamically applied behavior prompts aligned with detected traits.
Evaluation Method. Conversation quality was assessed using a structured Evaluation
Matrix scored by a custom Evaluator GPT system . Each assistant‚Äôs response was rated on
detection accuracy, regulation effectiveness, emotional tone, relevance, and satisfaction of
personality-specific emotional needs.
Results. Regulated assistants consistently outperformed baseline counterparts across
all criteria and both personality types, achieving a 34.44% average performance improvement.
Qualitative analysis confirmed that regulated assistants generated more empathetic and
contextually aligned responses.

1. Introduction
1.1 Problem Context
Psychosocial Risks of Loneliness in Aging Populations. Social isolation and
loneliness are increasingly prevalent among older adults, with profound effects on emotional
wellbeing, cognitive health, and overall quality of life. Studies indicate that over half of elderly
individuals report persistent feelings of loneliness (Musich et al., 2015; MacLeod et al., 2018), a
condition associated with increased risks of depression, cognitive impairment, and mortality
(Couture, 2012; Luo et al., 2012). Structural factors such as single-person households, chronic
illness, and mobility limitations further compound vulnerability, especially in aging societies like
Switzerland, where up to one-third of elderly residents report social disconnection (H√§mmig,
2019).
Limitations of Traditional Support Systems. Conventional interventions,
community programs, in-person therapy, or family support‚Äîare resource-intensive and not
consistently accessible. Technological alternatives, such as digital platforms and AI companions,
offer scalable, continuous interaction capabilities, especially for those facing mobility constraints
or living alone (Anttila et al., 2020; Marottoli et al., 2007). However, effectiveness varies based
on how well such systems recognize and respond to users‚Äô nuanced emotional and psychological
needs.
Emergence of Personality-Aware Conversational AI. Advances in large language
models (LLMs) have enabled conversational AI systems to simulate social interaction. Tools like

1.2 Theoretical Framework and Technological Foundations
Big Five Personality Trait Model. The Five-Factor Model (OCEAN: Openness,
Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) is a foundational framework in

disposition across emotional and behavioral dimensions, offering a logical basis for adaptive
interaction design.

Scalability and Automation Potential. The system architecture (Figure A.1.)

2. Background and Literature Review
2.1 Project Scope
Objective of the Study. This thesis investigates the effectiveness of
personality-adaptive behavior regulation in enhancing the emotional support capabilities of AI
assistants. It extends beyond preliminary feasibility testing to implement and evaluate a real-time
detection-regulation loop using the Big Five personality framework.
System Implementation Focus. The study focuses on building an adaptive chatbot
system that infers the user‚Äôs personality dynamically across conversation turns and adjusts its
tone and behavioral style accordingly. It simulates interactions with extreme personality types
(high-OCEAN and low-OCEAN) and evaluates system performance using a structured
Evaluation Matrix scored by a custom Evaluator GPT.
Application Context. The assistant is targeted at older adults at risk of social
isolation, aiming to deliver scalable emotional support through psychologically grounded,
dynamically modulated dialogue. The experiment‚Äôs design allows for precise control over
interaction variables, enabling traceable evaluation of personalization effects.

2.2 Research Objectives
Primary Objective. To evaluate whether real-time personality-adaptive regulation
improves conversational quality, emotional alignment, and psychological attunement in
chatbot-user interactions compared to static, non-adaptive baselines.

Personality-Aware Emotional Support AI. A conversational agent that dynamically
adapts its behavior in alignment with the user‚Äôs personality profile, with the goal of enhancing

Behavior Regulation. The process by which AI-generated responses are modified in
real time based on user personality traits. This includes tone, topic framing, emotional intensity,

2.4 Research Scope and Delimitations
Experimental Boundaries. The study focuses on short-form text-based interactions
(six turns per dialogue) with two simulated user profiles representing the extreme ends of the Big
Five personality spectrum. It does not attempt to generalize findings across live or longitudinal
user populations.
Technical Constraints. Interactions were conducted using a controlled system
architecture with fixed trait detectors and curated prompts. No voice, facial, or physiological data
were used; personality detection was performed solely through language-based input. Evaluation
was limited to textual content and was conducted via an LLM-based Evaluator OpenAI GPT
without human raters.
Exclusions. The study does not explore cross-cultural differences in personality
expression, user demographic variation beyond age category, or hybrid multimodal interaction
contexts. Additionally, ethical considerations such as user consent, data security, or emotional
dependency risk were acknowledged but not directly studied in this implementation phase.

2.5 Literature Review and Current State of Research
Challenges and Evolution in Affective Computing. Current AI models face
significant challenges in emotional recognition and personalization. Poor generalization across
individuals, contexts, cultures, and languages remains a notable limitation (Alisamir & Ringeval,
2021). Algorithms often struggle with subtle or complex emotions, particularly in nuanced
human interactions (Qasim, 2022). The field of affective computing has evolved beyond simple
emotion recognition towards simulation and stimulation, introducing new technical complexities
and ethical considerations, such as the need for interactional respect (Alberts et al., 2024).
Advancements with LLMs and Multimodal Systems. Recent progress in emotional
support assistants is largely driven by Large Language Models (LLMs) and multimodal
capabilities. Methodologies for building extensive emotional support dialogue datasets are
emerging (Zheng et al., 2023), alongside cascaded neural networks integrating LLMs with
dialogue state recognition to enhance emotional comprehension (Chen et al., 2023). Multimodal
systems like EmoAda combine various data streams (video, audio, text) to offer scalable
psychological support (Dong et al., 2024). Other innovations include Conversational Health
Agents (CHAs) interpreting multimodal cues for context-aware responses (Abbasian et al.,
2023), Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) like ARIEL monitoring emotional states for adaptive
support (Sorino et al., 2024), and physiology-driven LLMs using wearable data for stress
prediction and empathy (Dongre, 2024).
AI Companions and Digital Interventions for Loneliness. Specific applications like
AI companions (e.g., Replika, ElliQ) show potential in elder care, offering companionship and

4. Methodology
4.1 Overview
Experimental Design. The study employs a controlled experimental design
comprising two primary chatbot conditions. The Regulated Assistant dynamically adapts its
responses in real-time based on detected (Appendix B.4) user personality traits using
personality-specific regulation prompts (Appendix B.1). The Baseline Assistant (Appendix B.2)
delivers static, non-adaptive responses without considering user personality.
Personality Profiles. To effectively assess adaptive chatbot interaction capabilities,
two extreme personality profiles (Appendix B.3) were simulated from the Big Five model.
Personality Type A (Positive Extreme) exhibits maximum positive trait levels, while Personality
Type B (Negative Extreme) reflects maximum negative trait levels. Chatbots were specifically
configured as OpenAI GPTs (Appendix B.5, B.6) to interact as these clearly defined profiles.
Evaluation Framework. A systematic approach to conversation assessment was
adopted using an Evaluation Matrix (Appendix B.7). This matrix measures detection accuracy,
regulation effectiveness, appropriateness of emotional tone, relevance and coherence of
responses, and effectiveness in addressing personality-specific emotional needs.
Evaluator GPT System. Evaluations were conducted utilizing a structured Evaluator
GPT (Appendix B.8, B.9, B.10) designed explicitly for unbiased, incremental, and traceable
assessments of conversation quality. This system ensured rigorous comparative analysis between
adaptive and non-adaptive chatbot responses.

4.2 Experimental Setup
4.2.1 Personality Simulation Design
Personality Extremes Definition. Two extreme personality profiles were defined
using the Big Five (OCEAN) model. Personality Type A (Positive Extreme) corresponds to
OCEAN values (+1, +1, +1, +1, +1), representing users who are open, conscientious, extraverted,
agreeable, and emotionally stable. Personality Type B (Negative Extreme) corresponds to
OCEAN values (‚Äì1, ‚Äì1, ‚Äì1, ‚Äì1, ‚Äì1), representing users who are closed to experience,
disorganized, introverted, disagreeable, and highly neurotic.
Prompt Encoding. Each personality profile was systematically encoded into specific
prompts to simulate human conversational behavior. These prompts were carefully constructed to
maintain internal consistency across conversation turns, aiming at realistic portrayals of expected
behavioral responses for each extreme personality type.

Baseline Chatbots (Non-Adaptive). Five non-adaptive chatbot agents were also

personality traits.

with evolving user characteristics.

4.3.4 Cumulative and Dynamic Updating
Incremental Trait Refinement. The detection module uses a cumulative approach,
progressively updating personality assessments based on the entire dialogue history. Traits
remain neutral (0) until sufficient dialogue evidence clearly supports assigning a positive or
negative value (+1 or ‚Äì1). This approach aims to reduce premature trait classification, thereby
ensuring that chatbot responses align closely with observable dialogue patterns and interactions
within each specific conversation.

4.3.5 Integration with Behavior Regulation
Trait-Driven Adaptation. Upon updating the user's personality vector, the detection
module immediately transfers the profile to the behavior regulation engine. Each non-neutral trait
generates a trait-specific behavior prompt (detailed in Section 4.4.3), guiding the chatbot's
subsequent response regeneration. Traits marked as neutral (0) do not influence behavior at that
dialogue turn. This continuous detection-regulation feedback loop enables adaptive refinement of
the chatbot‚Äôs conversational style, accurately reflecting evolving user personality cues.

coherently, combining them into nuanced and harmonized responses rather than contradictory

6.3 Interpretation
Dynamic Adaptation Efficacy. The results robustly confirm the hypothesis that
dynamically adjusting chatbot responses to match user personality significantly enhances
interaction quality. Critically, improvements were consistently observed across extreme

Regulated chatbots effectively met varied psychological needs by adjusting interaction strategies

affiliation), validating the psychological foundations of the adaptive regulation mechanism.
Trait-Specific Adaptation Insights. Users characterized by high OCEAN traits
(Type A) benefited from exploratory, engaging, and empathetic interactions, effectively matching
their openness and sociability. Conversely, users with low OCEAN traits (Type B) received
interactions marked by careful emotional reassurance, structural guidance, and calmness,
precisely addressing their need for security and cautious engagement. These targeted adaptations
illustrate the flexibility and psychological depth provided by regulated assistants.

6.5 Limitations and Challenges
6.5.1 Methodological Limitations
No Real-User Validation. The study used simulated personalities based on idealized trait
extremes to ensure controlled experimental conditions, inherently limiting the generalizability of
results. While this design enabled precise control and reproducibility, it could not fully replicate
the dynamic, context-sensitive nature of authentic human interactions. Future research should
therefore include human experiments with actual users to verify and extend the current findings
within realistic conversational settings.

short-term (six-turn) dialogues, limiting understanding of long-term conversational dynamics.

Independence of Personality Traits. The system treated Big Five personality traits as
independent and orthogonal, simplifying trait detection and response regulation. However,
personality dimensions may potentially interact in more complex ways than this simplified
approach captures. For example, certain trait combinations could amplify or moderate
conversational behaviors beyond what isolated traits predict. Future iterations could explore these
trait interactions experimentally, enhancing the psychological realism and nuanced adaptability
of the chatbot's responses.

variations. Future implementations could benefit from incorporating more refined, multi-level
scoring systems, potentially improving the psychological granularity and precision of adaptive
responses.

6.5.2 Implementation and Design Challenges
Regulation Prompt Alignment. Initial attempts at mathematically combining trait scores
for behavioral prompts resulted in contradictory or incoherent interactions. To resolve this,
discrete, trait-specific prompts aligned explicitly with the Zurich Model were manually crafted
and validated to ensure psychological harmony.
Simplified Trait Simulation. Personality simulations were deliberately simplified,
focusing on clearly defined, fundamental behavioral indicators for each trait. This approach
provided clarity, traceability, and modularity within the experimental design, but inherently
limited ecological validity. Real-world personality expressions often involve more complex,
context-dependent interactions between traits. Future studies should therefore consider
incorporating more nuanced and composite personality simulations to better approximate
authentic conversational dynamics.

automated workflows to minimize human error.

broader personality spectrums or longer dialogues proved impractical within the manual
approach. Automated or semi-automated evaluation pipelines are recommended to enhance
scalability, reproducibility, and breadth of experimental coverage.

6.6 Implications
Theoretical and Practical Contribution. Despite limitations, the findings robustly
demonstrate the potential of personality-adaptive chatbot systems to significantly enhance
conversational quality. The Zurich Model-inspired regulation framework provides a theoretically
grounded and practical method for dynamically personalizing interactions, presenting valuable
implications for mental health, educational, and customer support applications.

7. Conclusion
Study Objectives and Findings. This thesis investigated the effectiveness of
dynamically adapting chatbot interactions based on detected Big Five personality traits compared
to non-adaptive baseline interactions. Through rigorous experimental design, systematic
evaluations via a custom Evaluator GPT, and alignment with Zurich Model motivational
principles, the study found substantial and consistent improvements across emotional tone,
coherence, and psychological alignment. Regulated assistants significantly outperformed baseline
assistants, achieving an average 34.44% performance increase across two simulated extreme
personality profiles.
Dynamic Adaptation Efficacy. Real-time personality detection and regulation proved
essential in dynamically refining conversational strategies, closely simulating natural human
adaptability. Trait-specific prompt design grounded in motivational psychology effectively
aligned chatbot behavior with fundamental psychological needs, enhancing user experience and
emotional resonance.
